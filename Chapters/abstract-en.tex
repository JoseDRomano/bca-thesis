Accurate classification of breast cancer subtypes is essential for enabling
personalized diagnosis and treatment strategies. However, the manual
stratification of patients based solely on clinical and pathological criteria
presents significant challenges, due to the molecular complexity and
heterogeneity of breast cancer. In this context, the integration of molecular
biomarkers such as microRNAs (miRNAs), small non-coding RNAs with key roles in
post-transcriptional regulation, offers a promising avenue for improving
diagnostic precision and the possibility for personalized treatments.

This dissertation investigates the capacity of classical machine learning to
classify the four PAM50 breast cancer subtypes from miRNA expression profiles.
A cross-paradigm framework combining supervised classification (XGBoost),
statistical feature ranking (ANOVA F-test), and unsupervised geometric
analysis (K-Means) was applied to two TCGA-BRCA cohorts (244 and
702 patients) to assess both performance and the replicability of identified
signatures.

A consistent separability gradient was identified and replicated across both
cohorts: Basal-like $>$ Luminal~A $>$ HER2-Enriched $\approx$ Luminal~B.
The Basal-like subtype was classified with near-perfect performance, while
HER2-Enriched and Luminal~B proved insufficiently separable from miRNA data
alone. Cross-paradigm and cross-cohort validation established a four-miRNA
panel: \textbf{MIR-577, MIR-934, MIR-18a, and MIR-135b}, as the most
robustly supported biomarker candidates, with MIR-577 consistently dominating
the discriminative signal across all methods and both datasets.

These findings delineate the scope and limits of miRNA-based subtyping and
provide a concrete, evidence-based panel for biological validation, currently
in progress at NOVA Medical School, towards targeted diagnostic tools for
Basal-like (triple-negative) breast cancer.
