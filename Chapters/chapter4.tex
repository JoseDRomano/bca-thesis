\section{Transition to the Larger Dataset}

All supervised and unsupervised experiments described in the preceding chapters were
developed, tuned, and evaluated on the \emph{pilot cohort}: the 244-sample, 462-feature
dataset assembled in Chapter~3 from a TCGA-BRCA data release that had been
pre-processed and curated by our partners at Nova Medical School prior to the start
of this project. This was the only dataset available at that stage of the work.
At a later point in the project, access was obtained to a substantially larger
and independently sourced dataset from the same TCGA-BRCA study, retrieved
directly from the cBioPortal for Cancer Genomics. The availability of this
expanded cohort created a natural opportunity to revisit the best-performing
pipelines developed on the pilot data and assess whether the findings generalise
to a larger and more representative sample of the same disease population.
This section documents the origin, composition, and relationship to the pilot
cohort of the expanded dataset that forms the basis of the analysis presented
in the remainder of this chapter.

% ============================================================
\subsection*{Data Source and Acquisition}
% ============================================================

The expanded dataset was obtained directly from the cBioPortal for Cancer Genomics
\cite{cerami2012cbio, TCGA2012}, an open-access platform that provides curated
multi-omics data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) \ref{TCGA-BRCA} and other large-scale consortia.
Specifically, the miRNA expression and clinical data for the TCGA Breast Invasive
Carcinoma (TCGA-BRCA) study were downloaded from the portal's data export interface.
Unlike the pilot cohort, which arrived as a pre-processed artefact from an established
collaboration, this release was obtained at a later stage in the project and represents a
direct, largely unprocessed export of the TCGA-BRCA repository. The clinical table and
the miRNA expression matrix were retrieved separately and subsequently merged by
patient identifier, as described in the preprocessing subsection below.
 
After integration, the raw combined dataset contained $N = 773$ samples described
by 539 miRNA expression features and 15 clinical variables, plus the PAM50 subtype
label. Following the application of the preprocessing steps documented in
Section~\ref{sec:expanded_preprocessing}, the final modelling cohort comprises
\textbf{702 samples}, making it approximately 2.9 times larger than the pilot cohort.

\subsection*{Assessing the Intersection with the Pilot Cohort}
% ============================================================

Because the two datasets were obtained through different channels and exhibit
differences in both nomenclature and feature composition (detailed in the next
subsection), it was necessary to formally verify that they originate from the same
underlying study before drawing any comparative conclusions. This verification was
conducted by cross-referencing the patient identifiers present in each dataset.

TCGA assigns every biospecimen a standardised barcode of the form
\texttt{TCGA-\{TSS\}-\allowbreak\{Participant\}-\allowbreak\{Sample\}}, 
where the first twelve characters
(\texttt{TCGA-XX-XXXX}) uniquely identify a patient, independent of sample type
or processing version. The pilot cohort uses the full barcode including the
two-digit sample code suffix (\textit{e.g.}, \texttt{TCGA-A1-A0SK-01}), while
the expanded dataset stores only the patient-level prefix
(\textit{e.g.}, \texttt{TCGA-A1-A0SK}). After normalising both sets of identifiers
to the twelve-character patient prefix, we can visualize in Figure~\ref{fig:ver_datasets} 
that \textbf{176 of the 244 patients in the pilot
cohort} (72.1\%) were found to have a direct match in the expanded dataset. The
remaining 68 pilot-cohort patients are absent from the expanded release, most likely
because they correspond to samples that were present in the specific TCGA data
version used by Nova Medical School but are not included in the cBioPortal export
retrieved at a later date, or because they were filtered out by the curation pipeline
applied at the source.

A 72\% overlap between an independently acquired dataset and a pre-processed
cohort sourced from the same TCGA study constitutes strong evidence that both
datasets describe the same patient population and the same underlying
disease characterisation effort. The patients who appear in the pilot cohort but
not in the expanded release can be attributed to version differences in the
TCGA data freeze or to curation decisions applied upstream, rather than to any
fundamental incompatibility between the two sources.

\subsection*{Differences Between the Two Datasets}
% ============================================================

Despite originating from the same repository and study, the two datasets differ in
several important respects. These differences are a direct consequence of the
independent preprocessing applied to each release, and understanding them is
necessary to interpret any comparative results.

\paragraph{miRNA Feature Space.}
The pilot cohort was built from a raw TCGA-BRCA miRNA expression matrix containing
888 features, which were reduced to 442 unique, non-redundant miRNAs after
the deduplication step described in Section~\ref{subsec:mirna_dedup}. The features follow a
HUGO gene symbol convention with isoform suffixes
(\textit{e.g.}, \texttt{MIR-744/5P}, \texttt{MIR-744/3P}).
The expanded dataset, by contrast, already provides 539 miRNA features in a
pre-deduplicated form, using the standard \texttt{hsa-mir-xxx} Ensembl/miRBase
nomenclature (\textit{e.g.}, \texttt{hsa-mir-744}). The difference in feature count
(442 vs.\ 539) reflects both the use of a different TCGA data release and the
different deduplication strategy applied at source: the cBioPortal export retains
one entry per unique miRNA base identifier, whereas the pilot cohort was filtered
by matching expression profiles across samples.
Although the two naming conventions refer to the same biological entities, a
direct one-to-one mapping between them is not guaranteed without an explicit
identifier translation step, as the cBioPortal identifiers represent precursor
miRNA genes while the pilot-cohort identifiers can encode strand-specific isoforms.

\paragraph{Clinical Feature Set.}
The clinical annotations available in the two datasets are largely complementary
rather than overlapping. The pilot cohort contains a rich set of molecular and
integrative features curated by Nova Medical School, including receptor status
indicators (\texttt{ER\_STATUS}, \texttt{PR\_STATUS}, \texttt{HER2\_STATUS}),
TCGA multi-omics cluster assignments
(\texttt{CN\_CLUSTER}, \texttt{METHYLATION\_CLUSTER}, \texttt{MIRNA\_CLUSTER}),
and detailed TNM staging variables. The expanded dataset, drawn directly from the
cBioPortal clinical export, provides a different profile of 15 variables centred on
genomic instability and tumour microenvironment metrics: the Aneuploidy Score,
three independent hypoxia scores (Buffa, Ragnum, and Winter), MSI
(Microsatellite Instability) scores from two platforms (MSI MANTIS and
MSIsensor), Tumour Break Load, and TMB (non-synonymous).
Both datasets share a common core of basic clinical descriptors: patient age at
diagnosis, tumour stage, lymph node involvement, distant metastasis, fraction of
the genome altered, mutation count, and TMB; which provides a minimal
comparable clinical basis across experiments. The key differences in feature
availability between the two datasets are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison}.

\subsection*{Characteristics of the Expanded Dataset}
% ============================================================

The final modelling dataset contains 702 samples described by 554 features: 539
miRNA expression values and 15 clinical variables. All features are fully numeric
and there are no missing values, as the preprocessing steps described below
removed samples or imputed values where necessary prior to modelling.

\paragraph{Subtype Distribution.}
As shown in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:expanded_subtype_dist},
the class distribution follows the expected epidemiological pattern, with Luminal A
comprising the largest fraction of the cohort ($n = 362$; 51.6\%), followed by
Luminal B ($n = 156$; 22.2\%), Basal-like ($n = 117$; 16.7\%), and HER2-enriched
($n = 67$; 9.5\%). Compared with the pilot cohort, the expanded dataset shows a
more pronounced Luminal A dominance (51.6\% vs.\ 42.2\%), which can partially
be attributed to the absence of the Normal-like class, which had been explicitly
excluded during the preprocessing of both cohorts. The Normal-like class, as seen 
in the smaller cohort, has a very low sample size that will unable our models and 
implementations to find a meaningful feature group that could represent this class.  
The class imbalance is less severe than in the pilot cohort in relative terms, but
it remains a relevant modelling challenge: a trivial majority-class classifier
would achieve 51.6\% accuracy without learning any discriminative signal. The same
class-imbalance mitigation strategies employed in the pilot study (stratified
cross-validation and class-weighted loss functions) are therefore retained in all
experiments described in this chapter.

\section{Data Pre-processing Pipeline}
\label{sec:expanded_preprocessing}

The expanded dataset was sourced as two separate files from cBioPortal: a miRNA
expression matrix (\texttt{mirna\_data\_filtered.csv}, 773 patients $\times$ 539
features) and a clinical annotations table (\texttt{clinical\_data\_filtered.csv},
773 patients $\times$ 21 columns). Both arrived in patient-as-rows orientation and
shared a common \texttt{Patient ID} key, so no transposition was needed before
merging (unlike the pilot cohort), where the miRNA matrix required structural
realignment (Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}).

\paragraph{Column removal.}
Four clinical columns were removed prior to joining: \texttt{Ethnicity Category}
(14.3\% missing), \texttt{Genetic Ancestry Label} (12.2\% missing), and
\texttt{Race Category} (6.5\% missing) due to substantial missingness, and
\texttt{Cancer Type Detailed} as a task-irrelevant field (all samples are
TCGA-BRCA primaries). This left 15 clinical variables for modelling.

\paragraph{Integration, subtype cleaning, and missing value handling.}
The two files were merged via an inner join on \texttt{Patient ID}. Since both
files contained the same 773 patients, the join introduced no sample loss.
The 26 Normal-like samples (\texttt{BRCA\_Normal}) were then excluded, leaving 747
samples. Residual missing values remained in three clinical features after this
exclusion: \texttt{MSI MANTIS Score} ($n = 24$), \texttt{Aneuploidy Score}
($n = 17$), and \texttt{Neoplasm Disease Stage} ($n = 4$). As in the pilot cohort,
listwise deletion was applied rather than imputation, given the clinical nature of
these variables. This removed a further 45 samples, yielding a final cohort of
\textbf{702 samples}.

\paragraph{Categorical encoding and miRNA renaming.}
The remaining categorical clinical columns (TNM staging codes, metastasis stage,
and lymph node stage) were label-encoded using \texttt{sklearn}'s
\texttt{LabelEncoder}. The PAM50 subtype was mapped to integers consistent with
the pilot cohort (Basal-like $\to$ 0, HER2-enriched $\to$ 1, Luminal A $\to$ 2,
Luminal B $\to$ 3). miRNA column identifiers were renamed by stripping the
\texttt{hsa-} prefix and capitalising \texttt{mir} to \texttt{MIR}
(\textit{e.g.}, \texttt{hsa-mir-577} $\to$ \texttt{MIR-577}) for readability.

\paragraph{Feature matrix and no-scaling decision.}
The final feature matrix $\mathbf{X}$ comprises 554 features (539 miRNA $+$ 15 
clinical) and the target vector $\mathbf{y}$ the integer-encoded subtype. The 
\texttt{StandardScaler} was applied on the continuous features: all 539 miRNA 
expression columns and the continuous clinical variables, while the 
integer-encoded ordinal and binary clinical columns were left unscaled as their 
values are already bounded and comparable in magnitude. Consistent with the 
data leakage prevention protocol described in Section~\ref{subsec:split}, the scaler was fitted 
exclusively on the training fold and subsequently applied without refitting to 
both the training and test folds.

\paragraph{Train/test split.}
The dataset was partitioned into an 80/20 stratified split, yielding 561 training and 141 test samples, with subtype proportions
preserved across both partitions, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_summary_expanded_BG}.

\section{Reassessing the Methodology at Scale}
\label{sec:expanded_pipeline}

The preceding sections of this chapter have established the foundations necessary to
carry the analysis forward. Section~\ref{sec:expanded_preprocessing} documented the
origin, composition, and preprocessing of the expanded cohort: a 702-sample,
554-feature dataset drawn independently from the TCGA-BRCA repository, encompassing
539 miRNA expression features and 15 clinical variables of a qualitatively different
nature from those available in the pilot study. The dataset is substantially larger,
differently annotated, and independently assembled and yet, it originates from the same
patient population where 72\% of the patients in the pilot cohort share the same identifier as the ones on this
studys.

With the data prepared and characterised, the natural question that follows is
whether the patterns identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:pilot} hold at this larger scale.
The pilot study produced two sets of findings that are worth reassessing. The first
is methodological: XGBoost under a One-vs-Rest formulation, validated against
ANOVA F-test feature rankings, proved effective both as a classifier and as a
biomarker discovery proxy. The second is biological: a consistent separability
gradient emerged across the four PAM50 subtypes, with Basal-like being the most
reliably identified and Luminal B the least, and with a small set of miRNAs -
MIR-577 prominently among them - accounting for a disproportionate share of the
model's discriminative gain for the most separable subtype. The unsupervised
K-Means analysis independently corroborated this picture, isolating Basal-like into
a high-purity cluster without any label supervision.

This section reapplies those same implementations - the XGBoost pipeline, the ANOVA
F-test sweep, and the K-Means clustering - to the expanded cohort without modification
to their architecture or parameterisation. Holding the methodology fixed is a
deliberate choice: the goal here is not to optimise performance on the new data, but
to test \textbf{generalisation}. If the separability gradient, the dominant feature
panels, and the unsupervised cluster structure are genuine properties of Basal-like
and the other PAM50 subtypes, they should be recoverable from an independently
assembled dataset of greater size. If they are not, the pilot findings would need to
be qualified as cohort-specific. The results of this reassessment form the empirical
basis for the cross-cohort comparison and discussion presented in
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}.

\subsection{Per-Subtype Classification Performance}
\label{sec:expanded_per_subtype}

The comparison tables presented above give a global picture of how the pipeline
behaves on the expanded cohort, but aggregate metrics can mask very different stories
playing out at the subtype level. Recall that the central motivation for using a
One-vs-Rest formulation is precisely to expose this variation: each binary classifier
is trained to distinguish one subtype from all others, and its performance directly
reflects how clearly that subtype's miRNA expression signature separates it from the
rest of the cohort. Understanding \textit{which subtypes are easy to identify and
which are not} is not merely a performance observation, it is a biological claim
about the geometry of the miRNA expression space, and it is the foundation on which
the feature extraction analysis in Section~\ref{sec:expanded_features} rests. A
subtype that is reliably classified is also a subtype whose most important features
are meaningful; a subtype that is not can only yield tentative biomarker candidates.

The following paragraphs examine each subtype in turn, connecting the pilot-cohort
findings from Section~\ref{sec:model-results} to the expanded-cohort results, so
that the reader can assess what held, what improved, and what remained a persistent
challenge.

% ==========================================
\paragraph{Basal-Like}
% ==========================================

In the pilot study, the Basal-like OvR classifier achieved perfect classification
under both feature configurations: precision, recall, and F1-score of 1.00 across
the board, which was attributed to the subtype's well-known and distinct triple-negative
miRNA signature. The natural question when moving to the expanded cohort is whether
this perfection was a function of the small pilot test set (only 8 Basal-like samples
in a 49-sample test split) or a genuine reflection of the subtype's separability.

On the 141-sample expanded test set (with 24 Basal-like cases) the answer is
unambiguous. In miRNA-only configuration, the classifier achieves precision 1.00,
recall 0.96, and F1-score 0.98; with clinical features added, it recovers to a
perfect F1-score of 1.00. The marginal drop in recall observed without clinical
data (four missed Basal-like samples out of 24) vanishes entirely when clinical
covariates are included. This performance, maintained across a nearly three-fold
larger test set, confirms that the pilot result was not a small-sample artifact:
Basal-like tumours occupy a geometrically isolated region of the miRNA expression
space, and the OvR classifier reliably finds it. As will be detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:expanded_booster}, MIR-577 remains the dominant discriminative
feature for this subtype across both conditions and both cohorts.

\section{Feature Extraction and Statistical Corroboration}
\label{sec:expanded_features}

The supervised classification results in the previous section establish that the
XGBoost pipeline generalises well to the expanded cohort, at least for subtypes with
strong transcriptomic signatures. However, strong classification performance is only a
means to an end: the model is used here as a \textbf{proxy} whose internal structure
can be interrogated to retrieve the miRNAs and clinical variables that carry the most
subtype-specific discriminative information. Accordingly, this section applies the
same two-level feature extraction and validation strategy developed in
Section~\ref{sec:booster-features} and Section~\ref{sec:anova-bridge}: per-subtype
gain importances from the XGBoost booster, followed by an independent ANOVA F-test
to assess whether the highest-ranked features are also \textbf{individually} and
\textbf{statistically} separable across subtypes.

As in the pilot study, both methods are treated as complementary rather than
redundant. XGBoost gain captures the incremental predictive value of a feature
\textit{within the model's multivariate decision process}; ANOVA F-test captures the
marginal statistical separation of a feature across subtype groups \textit{in
isolation}. A feature that is selected by both methods independently constitutes a
stronger biomarker candidate than one flagged by either method alone.

\subsubsection{Basal-Like Feature Panel}

The Basal-like gain importance landscape in the expanded cohort is dominated by
\textbf{MIR-577} to an even greater degree than in the pilot study. In the

\section{Group-Based Subtyping on the Expanded Cohort}
\label{sec:expanded_unsupervised}

In Chapter~\ref{ch:pilot}, the unsupervised clustering analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:unsupervised}) established that K-Means, operating without any
label information, recovers a data partition that aligns non-trivially with the known
PAM50 subtype structure. The Basal-like subtype, in particular, was consistently
isolated into a high-purity cluster in both the miRNA-only and miRNA + clinical
conditions. The expanded cohort provides an opportunity to test whether this implicit
structure in the miRNA expression space \textbf{persists and strengthens at scale}:
with almost three times as many samples, the geometric separation between
subtype-specific miRNA expression patterns should be more pronounced, provided that
the patterns are genuine and not noise-driven.

The same K-Means pipeline described in Section~\ref{sec:unsupervised:implementation}
was applied without modification: \texttt{StandardScaler} normalisation, $K = 4$
clusters (motivated in Section~\ref{sec:unsupervised:implementation} by domain
knowledge), K-Means++ initialisation, $n\_\text{init} = 10$ restarts, and fixed
random seed (\texttt{random\_state}~$= 42$). Both experimental conditions were
evaluated: \textbf{miRNA only} (539 features) and \textbf{miRNA + clinical}
(553 features). Labels were not used during clustering and were accessed only
post-hoc to evaluate purity and cluster composition.

The aggregate purity in the expanded cohort is virtually identical to the pilot
study under both conditions ($0.621$ vs. $0.611$ for miRNA-only; $0.623$ vs.
$0.598$ for miRNA + clinical), indicating that the degree of above-chance alignment
between the discovered clusters and PAM50 subtypes is \textbf{stable across cohort
sizes}. This stability is an important positive result: it suggests that the
clustering signal is not an artefact of the pilot cohort's particular sample
composition, but a consistent property of the TCGA-BRCA miRNA expression space.

The silhouette score, however, behaves differently across conditions. In the
miRNA-only setting, it increases from $0.069$ to $0.114$ on the larger cohort, 
a relative improvement of 65\%, indicating that the four-cluster partition is
\textit{geometrically more coherent} at scale, even if the overall purity is
unchanged. This is consistent with what one would expect if the Basal-like cluster
(which dominates the purity score) becomes more compact relative to between-cluster
distances as more samples fill in the expression landscape.

Conversely, in the miRNA + clinical condition, the silhouette score \textit{drops}
sharply from $0.068$ to $0.026$, while purity remains comparable ($0.623$). This
apparent contradiction, near-identical purity but near-zero silhouette, is
explained by the cluster structure detailed in the next section. The addition of 15
clinical features whose directions in feature space do not align with PAM50 subtype
boundaries is sufficient to distort the Euclidean geometry used by K-Means, creating
elongated or irregularly shaped clusters that are geometrically poorly separated but
still dominated by a single subtype. In short, clinical features help maintain
\textit{label alignment} but severely disrupt \textit{geometric compactness}, an
observation that reinforces the pilot study's conclusion that the
subtype-discriminating signal resides primarily in the miRNA expression space.

Two findings from Table~\ref{tab:kmeans_expanded_composition} warrant particular
attention.

\paragraph{Basal-like isolation is preserved and strengthened.} Under the miRNA-only
condition, a small but extremely pure cluster (C0, $n = 22$, purity 95.5\%) is
dominated by Basal-like samples. Under the miRNA + clinical condition, this isolation
becomes perfect: C0 ($n = 10$, purity 100\%) contains exclusively Basal-like samples.
The fact that K-Means, operating without labels, spontaneously isolates Basal-like
tumours with near-perfect purity on a 702-sample cohort constitutes strong evidence
that the Basal-like miRNA expression signature occupies a \textbf{geometrically
isolated region} of the expression space, a structural property rather than a
classifier artefact.

\paragraph{Luminal A dominates the large clusters.}
In the miRNA-only condition, C1 ($n = 428$, purity 73.1\%) is predominantly
Luminal A, forming the largest and most coherent cluster after the Basal-like one.
With clinical features, this structure is partially disrupted and redistributed
across two large clusters (C2: 54\% Luminal A, $n = 189$; C3: 77.3\% Luminal A,
$n = 317$), with C3 achieving a higher within-cluster purity. The emergence of two
Luminal A-dominated clusters under the combined condition may reflect the known
heterogeneity within Luminal A, as clinical features introduce additional axes of
variation that K-Means exploits to further subdivide the majority class.

\paragraph{HER2-enriched and Luminal B are not reliably isolated.}
Neither HER2-enriched nor Luminal B forms a high-purity cluster in either condition.
HER2-enriched samples are distributed across multiple clusters, and Luminal B
achieves its highest within-cluster representation (39.4\%) in a mixed cluster (C2)
that also contains substantial Basal-like (34.5\%) and HER2-enriched (12.8\%)
fractions. This lack of isolation is perfectly consistent with the supervised
analysis: both subtypes are characterised by diffuse and overlapping miRNA expression
patterns that do not map to geometrically compact regions in the standardised feature
space. K-Means cannot recover what the supervised classifier also struggles to
separate.

\subsection{Cluster Feature Signatures}
\label{sec_expanded_unsupervised_importance}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

For each cluster, the miRNAs that most strongly characterise its centroid relative to
the rest of the data were extracted using the absolute Z-score importance method
described in Section~\ref{sec_unsupervised_importance}. The top-20 features for each
cluster and both conditions are presented below. The interpretation focuses on the two
clusters with clear subtype identity (Basal-like and the dominant Luminal A cluster),
as these are the only cases where a biological interpretation of the centroid is
meaningful.

\paragraph{Basal-like cluster (miRNA only, C0).}
The top features for the Basal-like cluster (Figure~\ref{fig:bc_unsupervised_top20_c0_mirna}) 
include MIR-590 ($Z = 3.42$), MIR-18a
($Z = 3.30$), MIR-577 ($Z = 3.29$), MIR-130b ($Z = 3.05$), MIR-576 ($Z = 2.94$)
and MIR-197 ($Z = 2.86$). The overlap between this unsupervised centroid signature
and the XGBoost gain importances for Basal-like, in particular the consistent
appearance of MIR-577 and MIR-18a across both methods, provides cross-paradigm
corroboration for these miRNAs as Basal-like discriminators.

\paragraph{Basal-like cluster (miRNA + clinical, C0).}
With clinical features (Figure~\ref{fig:bc_unsupervised_top20_c0_clinical}), the 
Basal-like centroid signature is largely consistent with
the miRNA-only case: MIR-577 ($Z = 5.88$), MIR-576 ($Z = 4.96$), MIR-18a
($Z = 4.70$), MIR-590 ($Z = 4.51$), MIR-4766 ($Z = 4.50$) and MIR-130b
($Z = 4.37$) dominate. The increased Z-score magnitudes suggest that the Basal-like
centroid is even more clearly separated from the rest of the data in the combined
feature space, consistent with the 100\% purity of this cluster.

\paragraph{Dominant Luminal A cluster (miRNA only, C1).}
The Luminal A-dominated cluster C1 shows a much less concentrated signature. The
top features have markedly lower Z-scores ($\leq 0.47$), reflecting the absence of a
small set of dominant Luminal A-specific miRNAs. The leading features include
MIR-197, MIR-324, MIR-1d, MIR-1306, and MIR-33a - a distinct set from the XGBoost
gain importances for Luminal A, suggesting that different aspects of the Luminal A
miRNA expression landscape are captured by the centroid geometry versus the
multivariate decision boundary.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:expanded_unsupervised_summary}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

The K-Means analysis on the expanded cohort confirms and extends the pilot study's
findings on three key points. First, the \textbf{Basal-like subtype occupies a
geometrically isolated region} of the miRNA expression space: K-Means recovers a
near-pure Basal-like cluster without any label supervision, and this isolation becomes
perfect when clinical features are added. Second, the \textbf{clustering purity is
stable across cohort sizes}: expanding from 244 to 702 samples does not meaningfully
change the proportion of correctly isolated samples, suggesting the result is not
cohort-size-dependent. Third, \textbf{HER2-enriched and Luminal B remain inseparable
by unsupervised means}: neither subtype forms a high-purity cluster under any
condition, directly mirroring their difficulty in the supervised setting and
reinforcing the conclusion that their miRNA signatures lack the geometric compactness
required for cluster recovery.

The convergence between the supervised (XGBoost, ANOVA) and unsupervised (K-Means)
perspectives - centred on the same core miRNAs (particularly MIR-577 and MIR-18a)
for the Basal-like subtype, and on the same separability gradient across subtypes -
is the strongest methodological finding of this chapter, as it is fully independent
of any labelled supervision. The detailed cross-cohort comparison of feature panels
is reserved for Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}, where the results of both the pilot and
expanded analyses are synthesised in the light of the broader literature.

\section{Supervised and Unsupervised Perspectives: A Cross-Paradigm Comparison}
\label{sec:expanded_cross_paradigm}

The preceding sections approached the expanded cohort from two fundamentally different
angles. The supervised analysis asked: \textit{given that we know the subtype labels,
which features does a model need to classify each subtype correctly, and are those
features statistically meaningful in isolation?} The unsupervised analysis asked
instead: \textit{ignoring the labels entirely, does the miRNA expression data
spontaneously organise into groups that correspond to the known biology?} Because
the two paradigms make no shared assumptions whatsoever, any
finding that emerges consistently from both carries an evidential weight that neither
method alone could establish. This section brings those two perspectives together,
examining where they agree, where they diverge, and what those convergences and
divergences reveal about each subtype.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Basal-Like: Convergent Evidence from Both Paradigms}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

Basal-like is the subtype around which the supervised and unsupervised results
converge most completely. On the supervised side, the OvR XGBoost classifier achieved
a near-perfect F1-score of 0.98 in the miRNA-only condition and a perfect 1.00 with
clinical features, with MIR-577 alone accounting for 46.6\% and 64.2\% of the total
booster gain respectively. The ANOVA sweep independently confirmed that Basal-like
features carry the highest marginal statistical separation across all subtypes, with
the best cross-validated F1-score reaching 0.947. On the unsupervised side, K-Means
isolated Basal-like into a cluster of 95.5\% purity (miRNA-only) that became
perfectly pure (100\%) when clinical features were added, without ever observing a
single label.

Beyond these aggregate metrics, the feature-level agreement is particularly striking.
MIR-577 and MIR-18a appear as top-ranked features in the XGBoost booster importances,
in the ANOVA top-20 list (Figure~\ref{fig:anova_top_10_features_mirna_png}) and 
in the centroid Z-score signature of the
Basal-like K-Means cluster, under both feature configurations and across both cohorts.
This triple-method, dual-cohort replication places these two miRNAs in the strongest
possible position as biomarker candidates: they are simultaneously important to a
multivariate classifier, statistically separating in isolation, and geometrically
defining of the Basal-like cluster in expression space. The conclusion that emerges
from both paradigms is the same and reinforces each other: \textbf{Basal-like tumours
occupy a distinct, compact, and well-defined region of the miRNA expression landscape},
and that region can be navigated by both supervised and unsupervised means.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Luminal A: Consistent Performance, Inconsistent Geometry}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

Luminal A presents a more nuanced picture. The supervised analysis classified it
reliably, macro-averaged F1-score of 0.89 in the miRNA-only condition, recall of
0.96 and identified MIR-590 as the leading feature by XGBoost gain 
(Figure~\ref{fig:bc_xgboost_importances_mirna_png}), confirmed by
the ANOVA sweep. These results suggest a learnable signal. However, the unsupervised
analysis complicates this reading: K-Means never isolates Luminal A into a single
high-purity cluster. In the miRNA-only condition it is spread across two clusters
(C1: 73.1\% purity, $n = 428$; C3: 44.9\% purity, $n = 49$) 
(Figure~\ref{fig:bc_kmeans_purity_heatmaps_combined}), and with clinical
features it splits further into two large, Luminal A-dominated clusters of different
purities (C3: 77.3\%, $n = 317$; C2: 54.0\%, $n = 189$).

The apparent contradiction, namely reliable supervised classification but no clean
unsupervised isolation, is actually informative. It reflects the fundamental
difference between what the two methods are measuring. The OvR classifier learns a
\textit{decision boundary} that separates Luminal A from the union of all other
subtypes; it does not require Luminal A to be internally homogeneous, only that the
boundary can be drawn. K-Means, by contrast, requires Luminal A to form a
\textit{compact, convex region} in the standardised feature space, a considerably
stronger geometric demand. The fact that K-Means repeatedly splits Luminal A into
two sub-clusters, rather than conflating it with another subtype, suggests that the
subtype harbours genuine internal heterogeneity: there may be two biologically
distinct sub-populations within the Luminal A label that the PAM50 classification
does not resolve. The K-Means centroid features for these two Luminal A-dominated
clusters diverge somewhat from those identified by the supervised analysis,
reinforcing the idea that the unsupervised partition is capturing a different
dimension of the data's structure.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{HER2-Enriched and Luminal B: Consistent Difficulty Across Both Paradigms}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

HER2-enriched and Luminal B are the two subtypes where the supervised and
unsupervised perspectives tell the same negative story. From the supervised side,
the OvR classifiers for both subtypes achieved substantially lower F1-scores than
Basal-like and Luminal A, 0.42 and 0.56 respectively in the miRNA-only condition (Figure~\ref{fig:bc_xgboost_importances_mirna_png}),
despite access to labelled training data and a model explicitly optimised to find
discriminative boundaries. The ANOVA sweep produced the lowest best F1-scores for
these two subtypes (0.676 for HER2-enriched; 0.555 for Luminal B) (Figure~\ref{fig:anova_top_10_features_mirna_png}), and the feature
importance structures were diffuse: the top-20 XGBoost features (Figure~\ref{fig:her2_xgboost_importances_png}) accounted for only
57.1\% and 43.0\% of the total booster gain respectively in the miRNA-only condition,
compared to 92.0\% for Basal-like.

From the unsupervised side, neither subtype formed a high-purity cluster in any
condition. HER2-enriched samples were distributed across all four clusters in both
conditions, and the best within-cluster Luminal B representation reached only 39.4\%
(Figure~\ref{fig:bc_kmeans_purity_heatmaps_combined}) in a cluster also heavily populated by Basal-like (34.5\%) and HER2-enriched
(12.8\%) samples. K-Means, operating without labels, cannot find what the supervised
classifier (operating with labels) also struggles to find.

The alignment between the two paradigms here is not a failure but a finding.
It rules out the possibility that the supervised classifier's limited performance is
a model-specific limitation that a different algorithm might overcome: if the signal
were there in the miRNA expression geometry, K-Means would detect it as a compact
cluster, as it does for Basal-like. The convergent evidence instead points to a
data-intrinsic property, the miRNA expression profiles of HER2-enriched and Luminal B
\textbf{do not occupy geometrically separable regions} of the expression space, meaning 
that neither supervised nor unsupervised approaches can cleanly recover them from
miRNA data alone. For HER2-enriched, the addition of clinical features (specifically the
Buffa Hypoxia Score) provides a partial improvement in supervised classification,
suggesting that information orthogonal to the miRNA space exists but is not sufficient
to resolve the problem fully. For Luminal B, the primary limitation is its
transcriptomic proximity to Luminal A, a proximity that both the supervised decision
boundary and the unsupervised cluster geometry reflect consistently.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Role of Clinical Features: A Divergent Effect Across Paradigms}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

One of the more nuanced findings to emerge from the cross-paradigm comparison
concerns the effect of adding clinical features. In the supervised setting, clinical
covariates improve classification for HER2-enriched ($+0.15$ F1) and Luminal B
($+0.09$ F1), with negligible change for Basal-like and Luminal A. In the ANOVA
sweep, the clinical features yield meaningful F1 gains for HER2-enriched ($+0.042$)
and Luminal B ($+0.129$), again leaving Basal-like essentially unchanged.

In the unsupervised setting, the effect of clinical features is quite different.
Overall purity changes little ($+0.002$ from 0.621 to 0.623), but the silhouette
score collapses from 0.114 to 0.026, a near-fourfold drop in geometric coherence.
The structural consequence is visible in the cluster composition: the addition of
clinical features converts the clean Luminal A majority cluster seen under miRNA-only
conditions into two large, overlapping Luminal A-dominated clusters, while the
Basal-like cluster shrinks from 22 samples to 10 but becomes perfectly pure. The
clinical features are effectively introducing axes of variation that K-Means exploits
to subdivide the data, but those axes do not align with subtype boundaries - they
pull the clusters apart geometrically without improving their biological coherence.

The contrast is sharp and interpretable: \textbf{clinical features carry
discriminative signal that a supervised model can leverage against a labelled
boundary, but they introduce geometric noise that disrupts the unsupervised
structure}. This asymmetry is a direct consequence of how the two methods use
information. A supervised classifier learns \textit{which} dimensions matter for
each label; an unsupervised algorithm treats all dimensions symmetrically. In a
feature space where miRNA dimensions encode subtype structure and clinical dimensions
encode orthogonal biological variation, the supervised approach correctly weights the
informative dimensions while K-Means is pulled in multiple directions at once. This
distinction has a practical implication for biomarker discovery: clinical features
are more valuable as supervised covariates than as clustering inputs, and the miRNA
expression space is the more reliable substrate for unsupervised subtype recovery.
