\subsection{Data Source}
\label{subsec:data_source}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The dataset used in this work originates from \textbf{The Cancer Genome Atlas
Breast Invasive Carcinoma} (TCGA-BRCA) project, a large-scale, publicly available
multi-omics repository maintained by the National Cancer Institute. TCGA-BRCA
represents one of the most comprehensively characterised breast cancer cohorts in
the biomedical literature, providing standardised genomic, transcriptomic, and
clinical data for a large cohort of patients profiled with uniform experimental
protocols. Its public availability, scale, and annotation quality make it the
canonical benchmark dataset for computational breast cancer subtyping studies,
and it has been used as the primary data source in the majority of related work
discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:background}.

Two complementary data releases were obtained from the TCGA-BRCA portal and
used jointly throughout this work: a \textbf{microRNA expression matrix},
containing quantified expression levels for 888 miRNA features across 272
patient samples, and a \textbf{clinical data table}, containing 37 variables per
patient across a total of 463 records. These two sources are described in detail
in Sections~\ref{subsec:clinical_data} and~\ref{subsec:mirna_data} respectively.
Their integration into a unified modelling dataset is described as part of the
preprocessing pipeline in Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Working Cohort}
\label{subsec:working_cohort}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The full clinical release contains records for 463 patients. However, not all
patients in the clinical table have a corresponding miRNA expression profile
available in the molecular data release, which will drastically decrease our 
working cohort given that our objective is to find meaningful associations between 
\gls{mirna}, (potentionally) clinical features and subtypes of \gls{bc} . 
After restricting the cohort to
individuals for whom matched miRNA expression profiles existed, a \textbf{working
cohort of 272 samples} was obtained with the intersection of the two data sources.
All 272 samples correspond to primary solid tumour tissue
(\texttt{SAMPLE\_TYPE = Primary}) from mostly female patients (with over 99\% representation)
, consistent with the epidemiology of breast cancer as a predominantly female disease.

The class distribution reveals a pronounced imbalance that is characteristic
of breast cancer populations in clinical and research settings. Luminal A is
the dominant subtype, comprising 117 of 272 patients (43.0\%), while
Normal-like is severely underrepresented with only 5 samples (1.8\%). This
asymmetry has direct consequences for model training and evaluation: a naive
classifier that always predicts Luminal A would achieve over 43\% accuracy
without learning anything about the underlying biology. The modelling decisions
described in Section~\ref{sec:supervised} are informed by this imbalance, and
stratified sampling is used throughout to ensure that each experimental fold
preserves the original class proportions.

The 20 retained features span a broad range of clinical and molecular
dimensions. The three receptor statuses, \texttt{ER\_STATUS},
\texttt{PR\_STATUS}, and \texttt{HER2\_STATUS}, are the canonical markers
used in clinical breast cancer diagnosis and are intrinsically aligned with the
PAM50 subtype labels: for example, the Basal-like subtype is largely composed
of triple-negative tumours (ER$^-$/PR$^-$/HER2$^-$), a well-documented
biological correspondence that provides a natural consistency check for
classifier outputs. The TNM staging variables (\texttt{TUMOR\_STAGE},
\texttt{NODES}, \texttt{METASTASIS}) encode the anatomical extent of the
disease at diagnosis, while the genomic variables (\texttt{MUTATION\_COUNT},
\texttt{TMB\_NONSYNONYMOUS}, \texttt{FRACTION\_GENOME\_ALTERED}) reflect
tumour mutational characteristics that may (or may not) correlate with subtype-specific
oncogenic programmes. The integrative cluster assignments
(\texttt{CN\_CLUSTER}, \texttt{METHYLATION\_CLUSTER}, \texttt{MIRNA\_CLUSTER})
are derived from TCGA's own multi-omics analyses and encode cross-modal
molecular patterns that may complement the raw miRNA expression signal.

A descriptive overview of selected clinical characteristics within the working
cohort is as follows. Patient ages range from 26 to 90 years, with a mean of
$59.3 \pm 13.3$ years (median = 60), consistent with the known epidemiology of
breast cancer as predominantly a post-menopausal disease. Oestrogen receptor
positivity was recorded in 77.2\% of patients ($n = 210$), progesterone receptor
positivity in 64.7\% ($n = 176$), and HER2 amplification in 13.6\% ($n = 37$).
These proportions are broadly representative of an unselected breast cancer
population. Tumour stage at diagnosis was dominated by T2 lesions (61.4\%,
$n = 167$), followed by T1 (19.5\%), T3 (12.9\%), and T4 (5.1\%). From a
survival perspective, 89.0\% of patients ($n = 242$) were alive at the time of
last follow-up, with 30 recorded deaths.

\subsection{MicroRNA Expression Data}
\label{subsec:mirna_data}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The miRNA expression matrix contains quantified expression levels for
\textbf{888 miRNA features} measured across the 272 patient samples in the
working cohort. Each entry $x_{ij}$ represents the expression level of
miRNA $j$ in patient $i$, where higher values indicate greater expression
abundance in the tumour tissue. This constitutes a canonical
\textbf{high-dimensional, low-sample-size (HDLSS)} setting: the number of
features ($p = 888$) greatly exceeds the number of observations ($n = 272$),
a regime that poses well-known challenges for statistical learning and motivates
the feature selection strategies explored throughout this work. This complexity 
can be visually appreciated in the heatmap shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mirna-heatmap}, 
which displays the expression patterns of a random subset of 50 patients and 50 miRNAs, 
with the PAM50 subtype associated for context.

\subsubsection*{The miRNA Redundancy Problem}

A critical characteristic of the raw miRNA expression data, which becomes
apparent upon close examination, is the presence of widespread feature
redundancy. The 888 features in the raw matrix do not represent 888
biologically distinct microRNAs. Instead, a large number of entries share the
same \textit{base identifier} - for example, \texttt{MIR-758/758},
\texttt{MIR-758/3P}, and \texttt{MIR-758/5P} all derive from the same
precursor microRNA (MIR-758), differing only in the strand arm or isoform
designation. Across the full matrix, 888 Hugo gene symbols map to only 442
unique base identifiers, meaning that 446 features are variants of a base
miRNA already present in the data.

More importantly, upon inspection of the actual expression values, many of
these variants are found to carry \textbf{identical expression profiles} across
all 272 patients - that is, for a given patient, \texttt{MIR-758/758},
\texttt{MIR-758/3P}, and \texttt{MIR-758/5P} all report exactly the same
numerical value. This redundancy is attributable to a fundamental limitation of
the sequencing and quantification technology used to generate the TCGA-BRCA
expression data: the platform lacks the resolution to distinguish between
closely related isoforms of the same precursor miRNA, and consequently assigns
the same measured abundance to all of them. From a machine learning perspective,
these duplicated features add no discriminative information whatsoever (they
are mathematically identical columns in the feature matrix) and including
them in the model would artificially inflate the apparent feature space while
introducing collinearity that could distort feature importance estimates.

This observation directly motivates one of the first and most consequential
preprocessing decisions in the pipeline: the identification and removal of
duplicate expression profiles, described in detail in
Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}. As reported in our published study
\cite{ourpaper}, this deduplication step reduces the miRNA feature count from
888 to 464, eliminating 446 non-informative redundant features while retaining
the full unique expression signal. The resulting feature matrix is both more
compact and more interpretable, with each remaining miRNA representing a
genuinely distinct molecular measurement.

\section{Data Pre-processing Pipeline}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

Before any learning algorithm was applied, both the clinical and miRNA expression
data underwent a structured pre-processing pipeline. This pipeline was designed
to produce a clean, fully numerical feature matrix while strictly avoiding data
leakage between training and test partitions. The full procedure is described in
the following subsections and is summarised schematically in
Figure~\ref{fig:preprocessing-pipeline}.

% --------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Handling Missing Values and Feature Selection}
\label{subsec:missing-outliers}

\subsubsection{Dataset Integration}

The pre-processing pipeline began by integrating the two data sources described
in Section~\ref{subsec:primary-cohort}. The miRNA expression matrix was first
transposed from its original feature-by-sample orientation
(888 miRNAs $\times$ 272 samples) to the conventional sample-by-feature layout
(272 $\times$ 888), and the row index was renamed to \texttt{sampleId} to
establish a common join key. The resulting expression table was then merged with
the clinical annotation table via an inner join on \texttt{sampleId}, yielding
an integrated matrix of 272 samples and 925 columns prior to any reduction step.

\subsubsection{Removal of Uninformative Clinical Features}

A first round of feature elimination targeted clinical variables that carry no
discriminative information for the classification task. Specifically, 15 columns
were removed on the grounds listed in Table~\ref{tab:dropped-cols}. Broadly,
these fall into four categories: \\
(i) \textbf{redundant identifiers} (\texttt{patientId}); \\
(ii) \textbf{zero-variance fields}, i.e.\ columns that
take a single value across all samples (\texttt{studyId}, \texttt{CANCER\_TYPE},
\texttt{CANCER\_TYPE\_DETAILED}, \texttt{ONCOTREE\_CODE}, \texttt{SAMPLE\_TYPE},
\texttt{SOMATIC\_STATUS}, \texttt{SAMPLE\_COUNT}, \texttt{SEX}); \\
(iii) \textbf{high-missingness fields}, where the proportion of missing values was
deemed too large to recover reliably (\texttt{RPPA\_CLUSTER} at
$\approx$24\% missing; \texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS\_NO\_EXP},
\texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS\_UNSUP\_EXP}, and
\texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS\_WITH\_PAM50} each at $\approx$25\% missing); \\
and (iv) \textbf{task-irrelevant derived features} (\texttt{SIGCLUST\_INTRINSIC\_MRNA},
a cluster label derived from mRNA rather than miRNA data, and
\texttt{SURVIVAL\_DATA\_FORM}, a near-zero-variance administrative field).

After the elimination of high-missingness columns, a small number of samples

in five fields: 
\begin{itemize}

    \item \texttt{METASTASIS\_CODED} ($n$\,=\,4),

    \item  \texttt{METASTASIS} ($n$\,=\,4).
\end{itemize}

Handling missing values in a dataset with a relatively small sample size is a
non-trivial challenge. Working with biological data adds further complexity:
the human body operates as an interconnected system of molecular processes,
and filling in missing clinical measurements through standard imputation methods
risks introducing artificial patterns that do not reflect any true biological
signal. Given that the affected rows represent only 2.9\% of the cohort, and
that no systematic structure was identified in the missingness, these samples
were removed via listwise deletion. This approach ensures that all models are
trained on complete and reliable observations, prioritising data integrity over
sample size - a trade-off that is well-established in biomedical machine
learning~\cite{sterne2009multiple}. The miRNA expression matrix itself contained
no missing values (see Section~\ref{subsec:mirna-profiles}), so this step
exclusively affected rows with incomplete clinical annotations.

Following listwise deletion, the working cohort comprised \textbf{264 samples},
with the PAM50 subtype distribution revised to: Luminal~A ($n$\,=\,114),
Luminal~B ($n$\,=\,73), Basal-like ($n$\,=\,43), HER2-enriched ($n$\,=\,30),
and Normal-like ($n$\,=\,4).

\subsection{Dimensionality and Feature Scaling}
\label{subsec:dim-reduction-scaling}

\subsubsection{The Curse of Dimensionality in the Context of This Dataset}

The final feature matrix ($264 \times 909$) exhibits a moderately
high-dimensional structure. When the 20 clinical features are excluded and only
the miRNA expression panel is considered, the ratio of features to samples
reaches $p/n \approx 3.4$, which can degrade the generalisation performance of
many classifiers and renders unregularised models prone to overfitting. This
motivates the use of both intrinsic regularisation (e.g.\ in Support Vector
Machines and penalised regression variants) and explicit feature selection
strategies, which are described in detail in Chapter~4.

\subsubsection{Feature Scaling and the Prevention of Data Leakage}
\label{subsubsec:leakage}

Many of the machine learning algorithms employed in this work - in particular
distance-based and gradient-based methods - are sensitive to the absolute scale
of input features. Raw miRNA expression values span a range of approximately
$[-2.72,\; 18.66]$ on the log$_2$ scale (see Table~\ref{tab:mirna-stats}),
while clinical features such as \texttt{MUTATION\_COUNT} and
\texttt{TMB\_NONSYNONYMOUS} operate on entirely different numerical scales.
Standardisation to zero mean and unit variance was therefore applied to bring
all features onto a common scale prior to model training.

A critical methodological requirement is that standardisation must not be
fitted on any data that will later be used for evaluation. Fitting a scaler on
the full dataset before splitting into training and test partitions would
constitute \textbf{data leakage}: the test set statistics would silently
influence the scaling parameters, producing an overly optimistic - and
therefore misleading - estimate of generalisation performance. To prevent this,
the following protocol was strictly enforced throughout all experiments:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The dataset was split into training and test partitions 
          \textit{before} any scaling was performed, using stratified sampling 
          to preserve the PAM50 subtype proportions in both partitions.
    
    \item A \texttt{StandardScaler} was fitted \textit{exclusively} on the 
          training fold, computing the per-feature mean ($\mu_{train}$) and 
          standard deviation ($\sigma_{train}$) from training samples only:
          \begin{equation}
              z = \frac{x - \mu_{train}}{\sigma_{train}}
          \end{equation}
          where $z$ represents the scaled feature, $x$ is the original value, 
          $\mu_{train}$ is the training mean, and $\sigma_{train}$ is the 
          training standard deviation.

    \item The scaler fitted on the training fold was then applied 
          \textit{without re-fitting} to transform both the training and the 
          test fold. This ensures that the test set remains an unbiased 
          representation of unseen data.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The Supervised Learning Framework}
\label{sec:supervised_small_cohort}

\subsection{From Biological Question to Machine Learning Problem}
\label{subsec:bio_to_ml}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{The Nature of the Problem}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The central biological question motivating this work can be stated concisely: 
given a patient's molecular and clinical profile, is it possible to automatically 
and reliably determine which breast cancer subtype that patient belongs to? At first 
glance, this may seem like a straightforward lookup task, but the underlying 
complexity is substantial. Breast cancer subtypes are defined by intricate combinations 
of gene expression patterns, receptor statuses, and clinical variables, none of which 
alone provides a clean discriminative signal. The challenge, therefore, is not simply 
to associate a label with a patient, but to find a robust decision boundary in a 
high-dimensional feature space that reliably separates four biologically distinct 
and clinically meaningful classes: \textit{Basal-like}, \textit{HER2-enriched}, \textit{Luminal A}, and \textit{Luminal B}.

This framing immediately suggests a \textbf{multi-class classification} formulation 
rather than a regression one. Regression is appropriate when the output variable is 
continuous and when distances between output values carry meaning — for instance, 
predicting a patient's age or tumour size. The PAM50 molecular subtypes, however, 
are discrete and unordered categories. There is no meaningful numerical distance 
between Luminal A and Basal-like; they are not points on a scale but rather distinct 
biological identities, each associated with a different prognosis, treatment response, 
and underlying molecular programme. Treating this as a regression problem would 
impose a false ordinality on the target variable, introducing a systematic bias into 
the learning process. Classification, by contrast, treats each subtype as an independent 
category and learns to discriminate between them without presupposing any ranking or 
distance structure among the classes.

Furthermore, the problem is inherently \textbf{multi-class} rather than binary. 
While binary classification frameworks — such as one-versus-rest decompositions — 
are sometimes applied in clinical settings to simplify decision boundaries, they 
fragment the problem artificially and lose the joint distributional information 
across all four subtypes. A native multi-class formulation allows the model to learn 
simultaneously from the full label structure, capturing interactions and overlaps 
between subtypes that binary decompositions would miss. This is particularly 
important in the breast cancer context, where Luminal A and Luminal B are biologically 
related subtypes that share several molecular characteristics, and where their 
discrimination represents one of the hardest classification challenges in the dataset.


% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{Why Supervised Learning}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Machine learning broadly encompasses three major paradigms: supervised learning, 
unsupervised learning, and reinforcement learning. The choice between them is not 
arbitrary, but it is dictated by the structure of the available data and by the nature 
of the question being asked. In this work, the availability of clinically validated 
PAM50 subtype labels for each patient in the TCGA-BRCA dataset is the decisive factor 
that makes supervised learning not only possible but the most appropriate choice for 
the classification task.

In the supervised learning paradigm, a model is trained on a dataset of input-output 
pairs $\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$, where $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is 
the feature vector for patient $i$ and $y_i \in \mathcal{Y}$ is the corresponding label. 
The model learns a function $f: \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}$ that minimises a 
loss over the training set and is expected to generalise to unseen examples. In our 
case, $\mathbf{x}_i$ is a high-dimensional vector combining miRNA expression values and 
clinical variables, and $\mathcal{Y} = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ corresponds to the four PAM50 
subtypes encoded as integers, Basal-like, HER2-Status, Luminal A and Luminal B respectively. 
The learning objective is to find $f$ such that $f(\mathbf{x}_i) \approx y_i$ as 
closely as possible for all $i$ in the training set, and to ensure that this mapping 
holds for new patients as well.

The existence of ground-truth labels is what distinguishes supervised from unsupervised 
learning. Unsupervised methods, such as clustering algorithms, do not use label 
information during training; instead, they discover structure inherent to the data 
distribution itself. While unsupervised exploration has its own scientific value 
(and is pursued separately in Section~\ref{subsec:unsupervised_small_cohort}), 
it cannot directly answer the question of whether a patient belongs to a clinically defined subtype, 
because those subtypes are not purely data-driven constructs as they are biologically 
and clinically validated categories. Supervised learning, by contrast, directly 
optimises for the task of correctly assigning these validated labels, making it the 
natural and principled choice for the classification objective.

It is also worth distinguishing supervised learning from reinforcement learning in 
this context. Reinforcement learning involves an agent learning through interaction 
with an environment via reward signals, and is primarily suited for sequential 
decision-making tasks. Breast cancer subtype classification is a static, batch 
learning problem, so there is no sequential interaction, no environment, and no 
reward signal to exploit. This method would also require a lot of computational 
and data that we, unfortunately, do not have access to due to the precision and 
nature of the tests needed to extract this type of information from cancer patients. 
The supervised paradigm, with its direct mapping from patient features to subtype 
labels, is therefore the correct abstraction for this problem.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{The Proxy Inference Strategy}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Perhaps the most important conceptual contribution of this supervised framework 
is the way in which the classification task is reinterpreted as an instrument 
rather than an end in itself. The ultimate scientific goal of this work is not 
simply to build a classifier that correctly assigns breast cancer subtypes - 
that alone, while clinically useful, would not advance the biological 
understanding of the molecular mechanisms distinguishing those subtypes. The 
deeper goal is to identify \textbf{which microRNAs are most discriminative for each subtype}, 
and to do so in a way that is grounded in data-driven evidence rather than prior 
biological assumptions.

This is where the concept of \textbf{proxy inference} becomes central. The idea 
is the following: if a well-performing discriminative model is trained on the full 
feature space and then interrogated about what it learned, the internal structure 
of that model - specifically, the importance it assigns to individual features - 
provides an indirect but quantitatively meaningful signal about which features 
carry discriminative information for each class. The classifier thus acts as a 
\textit{proxy}: it transforms the problem of identifying discriminative miRNAs 
into the problem of training a good classifier and reading off its feature 
importance scores. This two-stage reasoning is illustrated schematically as:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stage 1 — Discriminative modelling:} Train a high-performing 
    multi-class classifier on the combined miRNA and clinical feature space. 
    Validate its performance rigorously using cross-validation and multiple evaluation metrics.
    
    \item \textbf{Stage 2 — Proxy feature discovery:} Extract per-class feature 
    importance from the trained model. Identify the top-$k$ miRNAs most 
    responsible for each subtype's classification. Validate the sufficiency of 
    this reduced feature set by retraining and comparing against the full-feature baseline.
\end{enumerate}

This framework is sometimes referred to in the literature as 
\textit{post-hoc interpretability} or \textit{model-based feature selection}, 
and it has a well-established precedent in computational biology, where the 
high dimensionality of omics data makes exhaustive biological validation of all 
features impractical. The proxy inference approach is particularly well-suited 
to tree-based ensemble methods such as XGBoost, which naturally expose per-class 
feature contribution scores through their boosting structure, as will be discussed 
in Section~\ref{subsec:classifiers}.

A critical methodological requirement of this strategy is that the classifier 
must first achieve genuinely strong performance before its feature importances 
can be trusted as meaningful signals. A poorly performing model may assign high 
importance to features for spurious reasons (noise, overfitting, or dataset 
artefacts) rather than because those features are biologically discriminative. 
The evaluation protocol described in Section~\ref{subsec:evaluation} is therefore 
not merely a performance benchmark but a \textit{prerequisite} for the validity of 
the proxy inference step.

\subsection{Baseline Model Evaluation}
\label{subsec:baseline_models}
% ================================================================

Before committing to a single classification algorithm, a systematic comparison of 
several candidate models was conducted in order to establish a reliable performance 
baseline and to justify the choice of the primary model used in subsequent analyses. 
This initial evaluation included four classifiers spanning a range of model families 
and complexities: \textbf{Random Forest}, \textbf{XGBoost}, \textbf{Naive Bayes}, \textbf{Logistic Regression}, 
and \textbf{Decision Tree}. This breadth of candidates was deliberate - by including 
both simple and complex models, as well as both linear and non-linear approaches, 
it becomes possible to reason about whether the discriminative signal in the data 
requires non-linearity, whether ensemble methods offer genuine gains over single 
estimators, and whether simpler, more interpretable models are sufficient for the task.

All models in this baseline phase were evaluated on the same stratified train/test 
split described in Section~\ref{subsec:split}, using the preprocessed and normalised 
feature matrix containing both miRNA expression values and clinical variables. 
Performance was measured using the standard suite of classification metrics: accuracy, 
weighted precision, weighted recall, and weighted F1-score, as well as per-class 
precision, recall, and F1-score for each of the four PAM50 subtypes. Confusion matrices 
were also inspected to understand the spatial distribution of classification errors across subtypes.

the clinical data integration study, and the proxy miRNA biomarker discovery pipeline.

enabling cross-model validation of the feature importance findings and ensuring that the 
proxy inference conclusions are not artefacts of a single algorithm.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimisation of XGBoost}
\label{subsec:xgb_tuning}
% ================================================================

Having selected XGBoost as the primary model, the next step was to optimise its 
hyperparameters for the specific characteristics of this dataset --- namely, its 
relatively small sample size ($n = 195$ training samples), high dimensionality 
($d = 462$ features), and moderate class imbalance. The choice of hyperparameters 
in XGBoost is consequential: an overly complex model risks overfitting to the 
training distribution, while an insufficiently expressive one may fail to capture 
the non-linear miRNA interactions necessary for robust subtype discrimination.

The initial XGBoost configuration used in the baseline evaluation was a broad 
starting point (\texttt{n\_estimators=300}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.05}, 
\texttt{max\_depth=6}, \texttt{subsample=0.8}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree=0.8}), 
borrowed from common defaults. Two refined configurations --- referred to internally 
as \textit{alpha} and \textit{beta} --- were explored as part of the hyperparameter 
search, defined as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Alpha}: \texttt{n\_estimators=50}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.3}, \texttt{max\_depth=10}, \texttt{subsample=0.85}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree=1.0}
    \item \textbf{Beta}: \texttt{n\_estimators=50}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.1}, \texttt{max\_depth=10}, \texttt{subsample=0.75}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree=0.7}
\end{itemize}

Both configurations reduce the number of estimators relative to the initial baseline 
(from 300 to 50), reflecting the recognition that in a low-sample-size regime, a 
large number of shallow boosting rounds with a moderate learning rate can achieve 
better generalisation than many rounds with a slow rate. The reduction in \texttt{n\_estimators} 
also provides a regularisation effect, limiting the depth of the ensemble's memory of training patterns.

The key distinction between the two configurations lies in the learning rate and the 
subsampling strategy. The \textit{alpha} configuration uses a higher learning rate 
(0.3 vs. 0.1) and samples all features at each tree split (\texttt{colsample\_bytree=1.0}), 
making it more aggressive and potentially more expressive on the full feature space. 
The \textit{beta} configuration, with a lower learning rate and reduced column subsampling (0.7), 
introduces more stochasticity into the feature selection process at each split, which can act 
as an implicit regulariser and reduce sensitivity to correlated features --- a relevant 
consideration given the co-expression patterns present in miRNA data.

Several observations merit discussion. First, the \textbf{Basal-like} subtype 
achieves perfect classification (F1 = 1.00) under both settings, a result that 
is robust across multiple cross-validation configurations and consistent with 
the findings of the published study. This subtype possesses a highly distinct 
miRNA expression profile that is unambiguously separable from the other three 
classes, and no clinical features are needed to achieve this separation. 
Second, the \textbf{HER2-enriched} subtype presents the greatest classification 
challenge, with a recall of 0.67 indicating that one third of HER2-enriched 
patients are misclassified. This is expected given the small support of this 
class in the test set (6 samples) and the biological overlap between HER2-enriched 
and Luminal B, where receptor status patterns can partially overlap. Third, 
the \textbf{Luminal A / Luminal B} discrimination represents the canonical 
hard problem in PAM50 subtyping, and the model achieves reasonable but imperfect 
separation, with Luminal A benefiting from its larger support (21 test samples) 
while Luminal B, the smallest majority class, exhibits the lowest absolute F1 score.

The cross-validation performance, aggregated across all fold configurations, 
confirms that the single-split results are representative rather than optimistic. 
Macro F1-scores obtained through repeated stratified cross-validation consistently 
fall in the range reported above, with standard deviations typically below 0.06, 
indicating a stable model with limited sensitivity to the specific composition 
of the train/test partition. These results establish a rigorous and reproducible 
performance baseline from which the proxy miRNA biomarker discovery, described 
in the following section, can be conducted with confidence.
