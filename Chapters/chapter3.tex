\section{Data Acquisition and Characterisation}

\subsection{The Primary Cohort (\textit{N}\,=\,272): Source and Clinical Features}
\label{subsec:primary-cohort}

The dataset used throughout this work was obtained from The Cancer Genome Atlas
Breast Invasive Carcinoma (TCGA-BRCA) study, accessed via the cBioPortal for
Cancer Genomics~\cite{https://www.cbioportal.org/study/summary?id=brca_tcga_pub}
under the identifier \texttt{brca\_tcga\_pub}. TCGA-BRCA is one of the most
comprehensive multi-omics repositories available for breast cancer research,
providing harmonised clinical annotations, genomic profiles, and molecular
expression data for a large cohort of patients profiled with standardised
protocols.

The full clinical release contains records for 463 patients. After restricting
the cohort to individuals for whom matched miRNA expression profiles were
available (see Section~\ref{subsec:mirna-profiles}), a final working cohort of
\textbf{272 samples} was obtained. All samples correspond to primary solid tumour
tissue (\texttt{SAMPLE\_TYPE = Primary}) from female patients, consistent with
the epidemiology of the disease. The clinical data matrix comprises 37 variables
per patient, covering receptor status, tumour staging, survival outcomes, and
several integrative molecular annotations.

\paragraph{Molecular subtype labels.}
The primary classification target in this thesis is the \textbf{PAM50 intrinsic
subtype}~\cite{parker2009supervised}, a well-established gene-expression-based
taxonomy that partitions breast cancer into five biologically distinct subtypes.
Table~\ref{tab:bc_subtypes_summary} summarises the key biological characteristics
of the four main subtypes; Normal-like is the fifth but is omitted from that
summary due to its low prevalence in the dataset. The subtype distribution
within the working cohort is presented in Table~\ref{tab:pam50-distribution}.
Luminal~A is the dominant class (43\%), whereas Normal-like is severely
underrepresented ($n$\,=\,5; 1.8\%). This pronounced class imbalance is a
central challenge addressed in the modelling strategy described in Chapter~4.

\paragraph{Demographic and clinical characteristics.}
Patient ages range from 26 to 90 years, with a mean of 57.9\,$\pm$\,13.3 years
(median\,=\,58). The age distribution is approximately symmetric around the
sixth decade of life, consistent with the known epidemiology of post-menopausal
breast cancer~\cite{Postmenopausal_bca_Rosenberg2006Risk}.

Receptor status was assessed immunohistochemically for the three canonical
markers. Oestrogen receptor (ER) positivity was recorded in 76.5\% of patients,
progesterone receptor (PR) positivity in 65.9\%, and HER2 amplification in
14.7\%. These proportions reflect the expected distribution in an unselected
breast cancer population and align with the PAM50 subtype assignments: the
Basal-like subclass is largely composed of triple-negative tumours
(ER$^{-}$/PR$^{-}$/HER2$^{-}$), a well-documented biological correspondence
that provides a useful sanity check for classifier outputs.

Tumour stage at diagnosis was available for 271 patients: stage T2 was the most
frequent (57.9\%), followed by T1 (25.9\%), T3 (11.7\%), and T4 (3.7\%). From
an overall survival perspective, 87.5\% of patients ($n$\,=\,238) were alive at
the time of last follow-up, with 58 recorded deaths.

\paragraph{Missing data.}
Several integrative annotation fields - specifically the
\texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS} variables ($\approx$25\% missing) and the
\texttt{RPPA\_CLUSTER} field ($\approx$24\% missing) - contain substantial
proportions of missing values, reflecting the multi-platform nature of the
TCGA-BRCA data collection, where not every assay was performed on every sample.
Beyond these high-missingness columns, a small number of samples present
isolated missing values in fields such as \texttt{HER2\_STATUS},
\texttt{METASTASIS\_CODED}, and \texttt{TUMOR\_T1\_CODED}. The precise
treatment of all missing data is described in Section~\ref{subsec:missing-outliers}.
The PAM50 label, however, is complete across all 272 samples and was therefore
adopted as the sole ground-truth annotation for all supervised and unsupervised
experiments in this thesis.

\subsection{miRNA Expression Profiles: Data Distribution and Normalisation}
\label{subsec:mirna-profiles}

\subsubsection{Dataset Dimensions and Data Type}

The miRNA expression matrix was obtained from the same TCGA-BRCA release and
encompasses \textbf{888 miRNA features} measured across \textbf{272 patient
samples}. This is a canonical example of a \textit{high-dimensional,
low-sample-size} (HDLSS) setting, where the number of features ($p = 888$)
greatly exceeds the number of observations ($n = 272$), posing well-known
challenges for statistical learning that motivate the feature selection
strategies explored in Chapter~4.

\subsubsection{Upstream Processing and Normalisation}

The expression values delivered by the TCGA data portal had already undergone
upstream processing by the GDC harmonisation pipeline: raw sequencing reads were
aligned to the reference genome, per-miRNA read counts were quantified, and
values were normalised and log$_2$-transformed. The presence of negative values
in the matrix (6.3\% of all entries, minimum\,=\,$-2.72$) is consistent with a
log$_2$-transformed representation in which low-abundance miRNAs fall below the
baseline signal. No raw count data were therefore used; all analyses operate
directly on the log$_2$-transformed profiles provided by the GDC pipeline. Any
further transformations applied as part of the pre-processing pipeline - in
particular, feature standardisation and the prevention of data leakage - are
described in Section~\ref{subsec:dim-reduction-scaling}.

\subsubsection{Distributional Properties}

Global summary statistics for the expression matrix are reported in
Table~\ref{tab:mirna-stats}. The overall distribution is slightly right-skewed
(mean\,$>$\,median), reflecting a subset of miRNAs with very high expression
levels (14.1\% of values exceed 10 on the log$_2$ scale). No missing values
were present in the matrix, which eliminates the need for imputation.

\paragraph{Per-feature variance.}
Examining the distribution of per-miRNA standard deviations reveals substantial
heterogeneity in inter-patient variability across features. Standard deviations
range from 0.53 to 2.75, with a mean of 1.14. Notably, 37 miRNAs (4.2\% of the
panel) exhibit a standard deviation greater than 2.0, indicating a high degree
of inter-patient variability. These high-variance features are expected to carry
the greatest discriminative signal for subtype classification, a hypothesis that
is systematically investigated through the feature selection analyses presented
in sections \ref{sec:supervised_small_cohort} and \ref{sec:unsupervised_small_cohort}.

\section{Data Pre-processing Pipeline}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

Before any learning algorithm was applied, both the clinical and miRNA expression
data underwent a structured pre-processing pipeline. This pipeline was designed
to produce a clean, fully numerical feature matrix while strictly avoiding data
leakage between training and test partitions. The full procedure is described in
the following subsections and is summarised schematically in
Figure~\ref{fig:preprocessing-pipeline}.

% --------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Handling Missing Values and Feature Selection}
\label{subsec:missing-outliers}

\subsubsection{Dataset Integration}

The pre-processing pipeline began by integrating the two data sources described
in Section~\ref{subsec:primary-cohort}. The miRNA expression matrix was first
transposed from its original feature-by-sample orientation
(888 miRNAs $\times$ 272 samples) to the conventional sample-by-feature layout
(272 $\times$ 888), and the row index was renamed to \texttt{sampleId} to
establish a common join key. The resulting expression table was then merged with
the clinical annotation table via an inner join on \texttt{sampleId}, yielding
an integrated matrix of 272 samples and 925 columns prior to any reduction step.

\subsubsection{Removal of Uninformative Clinical Features}

A first round of feature elimination targeted clinical variables that carry no
discriminative information for the classification task. Specifically, 15 columns
were removed on the grounds listed in Table~\ref{tab:dropped-cols}. Broadly,
these fall into four categories: \\
(i) \textbf{redundant identifiers} (\texttt{patientId}); \\
(ii) \textbf{zero-variance fields}, i.e.\ columns that
take a single value across all samples (\texttt{studyId}, \texttt{CANCER\_TYPE},
\texttt{CANCER\_TYPE\_DETAILED}, \texttt{ONCOTREE\_CODE}, \texttt{SAMPLE\_TYPE},
\texttt{SOMATIC\_STATUS}, \texttt{SAMPLE\_COUNT}, \texttt{SEX}); \\
(iii) \textbf{high-missingness fields}, where the proportion of missing values was
deemed too large to recover reliably (\texttt{RPPA\_CLUSTER} at
$\approx$24\% missing; \texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS\_NO\_EXP},
\texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS\_UNSUP\_EXP}, and
\texttt{INTEGRATED\_CLUSTERS\_WITH\_PAM50} each at $\approx$25\% missing); \\
and (iv) \textbf{task-irrelevant derived features} (\texttt{SIGCLUST\_INTRINSIC\_MRNA},
a cluster label derived from mRNA rather than miRNA data, and
\texttt{SURVIVAL\_DATA\_FORM}, a near-zero-variance administrative field).

\subsubsection{Handling Residual Missing Values}

After the elimination of high-missingness columns, a small number of samples
still contained at least one missing value in the remaining clinical features.
Specifically, 8 of the 272 samples were affected, with missingness concentrated
in five fields: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{FRACTION\_GENOME\_ALTERED} ($n$\,=\,2),
    \item \texttt{HER2\_STATUS} ($n$\,=\,1),
    \item \texttt{METASTASIS\_CODED} ($n$\,=\,4),
    \item \texttt{TUMOR\_T1\_CODED} ($n$\,=\,3),
    \item  \texttt{METASTASIS} ($n$\,=\,4).
\end{itemize}

Handling missing values in a dataset with a relatively small sample size is a
non-trivial challenge. Working with biological data adds further complexity:
the human body operates as an interconnected system of molecular processes,
and filling in missing clinical measurements through standard imputation methods
risks introducing artificial patterns that do not reflect any true biological
signal. Given that the affected rows represent only 2.9\% of the cohort, and
that no systematic structure was identified in the missingness, these samples
were removed via listwise deletion. This approach ensures that all models are
trained on complete and reliable observations, prioritising data integrity over
sample size - a trade-off that is well-established in biomedical machine
learning~\cite{sterne2009multiple}. The miRNA expression matrix itself contained
no missing values (see Section~\ref{subsec:mirna-profiles}), so this step
exclusively affected rows with incomplete clinical annotations.

Following listwise deletion, the working cohort comprised \textbf{264 samples},
with the PAM50 subtype distribution revised to: Luminal~A ($n$\,=\,114),
Luminal~B ($n$\,=\,73), Basal-like ($n$\,=\,43), HER2-enriched ($n$\,=\,30),
and Normal-like ($n$\,=\,4).

\subsection{Dimensionality and Feature Scaling}
\label{subsec:dim-reduction-scaling}

\subsubsection{The Curse of Dimensionality in the Context of This Dataset}

The final feature matrix ($264 \times 909$) exhibits a moderately
high-dimensional structure. When the 20 clinical features are excluded and only
the miRNA expression panel is considered, the ratio of features to samples
reaches $p/n \approx 3.4$, which can degrade the generalisation performance of
many classifiers and renders unregularised models prone to overfitting. This
motivates the use of both intrinsic regularisation (e.g.\ in Support Vector
Machines and penalised regression variants) and explicit feature selection
strategies, which are described in detail in Chapter~4.

\subsubsection{Feature Scaling and the Prevention of Data Leakage}
\label{subsubsec:leakage}

Many of the machine learning algorithms employed in this work - in particular
distance-based and gradient-based methods - are sensitive to the absolute scale
of input features. Raw miRNA expression values span a range of approximately
$[-2.72,\; 18.66]$ on the log$_2$ scale (see Table~\ref{tab:mirna-stats}),
while clinical features such as \texttt{MUTATION\_COUNT} and
\texttt{TMB\_NONSYNONYMOUS} operate on entirely different numerical scales.
Standardisation to zero mean and unit variance was therefore applied to bring
all features onto a common scale prior to model training.

A critical methodological requirement is that standardisation must not be
fitted on any data that will later be used for evaluation. Fitting a scaler on
the full dataset before splitting into training and test partitions would
constitute \textbf{data leakage}: the test set statistics would silently
influence the scaling parameters, producing an overly optimistic - and
therefore misleading - estimate of generalisation performance. To prevent this,
the following protocol was strictly enforced throughout all experiments:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The dataset was split into training and test partitions 
          \textit{before} any scaling was performed, using stratified sampling 
          to preserve the PAM50 subtype proportions in both partitions.
    
    \item A \texttt{StandardScaler} was fitted \textit{exclusively} on the 
          training fold, computing the per-feature mean ($\mu_{train}$) and 
          standard deviation ($\sigma_{train}$) from training samples only:
          \begin{equation}
              z = \frac{x - \mu_{train}}{\sigma_{train}}
          \end{equation}
          where $z$ represents the scaled feature, $x$ is the original value, 
          $\mu_{train}$ is the training mean, and $\sigma_{train}$ is the 
          training standard deviation.

    \item The scaler fitted on the training fold was then applied 
          \textit{without re-fitting} to transform both the training and the 
          test fold. This ensures that the test set remains an unbiased 
          representation of unseen data.
\end{enumerate}
