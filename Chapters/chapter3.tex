\subsection{Data Source}
\label{subsec:data_source}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The dataset used in this work originates from \textbf{The Cancer Genome Atlas
Breast Invasive Carcinoma} (TCGA-BRCA) project, a large-scale, publicly available
multi-omics repository maintained by the National Cancer Institute. TCGA-BRCA
represents one of the most comprehensively characterised breast cancer cohorts in
the biomedical literature, providing standardised genomic, transcriptomic, and
clinical data for a large cohort of patients profiled with uniform experimental
protocols. Its public availability, scale, and annotation quality make it the
canonical benchmark dataset for computational breast cancer subtyping studies,
and it has been used as the primary data source in the majority of related work
discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:background}.

Two complementary data releases were obtained from the TCGA-BRCA portal and
used jointly throughout this work: a \textbf{microRNA expression matrix},
containing quantified expression levels for 888 miRNA features across 272
patient samples, and a \textbf{clinical data table}, containing 37 variables per
patient across a total of 463 records. These two sources are described in detail
in Sections~\ref{subsec:clinical_data} and~\ref{subsec:mirna_data} respectively.
Their integration into a unified modelling dataset is described as part of the
preprocessing pipeline in Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}.

\subsection{The Working Cohort}
\label{subsec:working_cohort}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The full clinical release and the miRNA expression data contain records for 463 
patients and 272 patients, respectively. However, not all
patients in the clinical table have a corresponding miRNA expression profile
available in the molecular data release, which will drastically decrease our 
working cohort given that our objective is to find meaningful associations between 
\gls{mirna}, (potentionally) clinical features and subtypes of \gls{bc}. 
After restricting the cohort to
individuals for whom matched miRNA expression profiles existed, a \textbf{working
cohort of 272 samples} was obtained with the intersection of the two data sources.
All 272 samples correspond to primary solid tumour tissue
(\texttt{SAMPLE\_TYPE = Primary}) from mostly female patients (with over 99\% representation)
, consistent with the epidemiology of breast cancer as a predominantly female disease.

The 20 retained features span a broad range of clinical and molecular
dimensions. The three receptor statuses, \texttt{ER\_STATUS},

\subsection{MicroRNA Expression Data}
\label{subsec:mirna_data}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The miRNA expression matrix contains quantified expression levels for
\textbf{888 miRNA features} measured across the 272 patient samples in the
working cohort. Each entry $x_{ij}$ represents the expression level of

the feature selection strategies explored throughout this work. This complexity 
can be visually appreciated in the heatmap shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mirna-heatmap}, 
which displays the expression patterns of a random subset of 50 patients and 50 miRNAs, 
with the PAM50 subtype associated for context.

\subsubsection*{The miRNA Redundancy Problem}

A critical characteristic of the raw miRNA expression data, which becomes
apparent upon close examination, is the presence of widespread feature
redundancy. The 888 features in the raw matrix do not represent 888
biologically distinct microRNAs. Instead, a large number of entries share the
same \textit{base identifier} - for example, \texttt{MIR-758/758},
\texttt{MIR-758/3P}, and \texttt{MIR-758/5P} all derive from the same
precursor microRNA (MIR-758), differing only in the strand arm or isoform
designation. Across the full matrix, 888 Hugo gene symbols map to only 442
unique base identifiers, meaning that 446 features are variants of a base
miRNA already present in the data.

More importantly, upon inspection of the actual expression values, many of
these variants are found to carry \textbf{identical expression profiles} across
all 272 patients - that is, for a given patient, \texttt{MIR-758/758},
\texttt{MIR-758/3P}, and \texttt{MIR-758/5P} all report exactly the same
numerical value. This redundancy is attributable to a fundamental limitation of
the sequencing and quantification technology used to generate the TCGA-BRCA
expression data: the platform lacks the resolution to distinguish between
closely related isoforms of the same precursor miRNA, and consequently assigns
the same measured abundance to all of them. From a machine learning perspective,
these duplicated features add no discriminative information whatsoever (they
are mathematically identical columns in the feature matrix) and including
them in the model would artificially inflate the apparent feature space while
introducing collinearity that could distort feature importance estimates.

This observation directly motivates one of the first and most consequential
preprocessing decisions in the pipeline: the identification and removal of
duplicate expression profiles, described in detail in
Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}. As reported in our published study
\cite{ourpaper}, this deduplication step reduces the miRNA feature count from
888 to 464, eliminating 446 non-informative redundant features while retaining
the full unique expression signal. The resulting feature matrix is both more
compact and more interpretable, with each remaining miRNA representing a
genuinely distinct molecular measurement.

Now that we have characterised the data sources and the working cohort, 
we can proceed to the pre-processing pipeline that transforms the raw data 
into a clean, numerical feature matrix suitable for machine learning. The next 
section describes this pipeline in detail, including the handling of missing 
values, feature selection decisions, and the critical steps taken to avoid 
data leakage between training and test partitions.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data Integration}
\label{subsec:integration}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The two raw data sources arrived in structurally incompatible orientations. The
miRNA expression matrix followed the conventional bioinformatics layout:
features (miRNAs) as rows and patients as columns, yielding a matrix of
$888 \times 275$ (where 3 of the 275 columns are metadata fields:
\texttt{entrezGeneId}, \texttt{hugoGeneSymbol}, and \texttt{type}). The
clinical data table, by contrast, followed the standard tabular layout with
patients as rows and variables as columns ($463 \times 37$). Before any join
could be performed, the miRNA matrix had to be transformed to match this
convention.
The transformation proceeded in four steps:

\begin{itemize}
    \item[(i)] the metadata columns \texttt{entrezGeneId} and \texttt{type} were dropped, retaining only the gene symbol and expression values;
    \item[(ii)] the matrix was transposed, placing patients on the rows and miRNAs on the columns;
    \item[(iii)] the first row of the transposed matrix (which contained the miRNA identifiers) was promoted to serve as the column header;
    \item[(iv)] the row index was renamed to \texttt{sampleId} to establish a common join key with the clinical table.
\end{itemize}

The two tables were then merged via an \textbf{inner join} on \texttt{sampleId}.
The inner join naturally restricts the combined dataset to patients for whom
both miRNA expression data and clinical annotations are available
simultaneously, yielding an integrated matrix of \textbf{272 samples} and
$888 + 37 = 925$ features prior to any reduction step. The 191 clinical
records without matched miRNA profiles were discarded at this stage.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{miRNA Deduplication}
\label{subsec:mirna_dedup}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

As described in Section~\ref{subsec:mirna_data}, the 888 miRNA features in
the raw matrix do not represent 888 biologically independent measurements.
A substantial fraction of entries share the same precursor base identifier,
for example, \texttt{MIR-758/758}, \texttt{MIR-758/3P}, and
\texttt{MIR-758/5P} (visually explained in Table~\ref{tab:mirna-variants}). These 
carry \textbf{identical numerical expression values} across all 272 samples.
This redundancy comes from the limited sensibility of the extraction technique 
used to collect these miRNA expression values which cannot distinguish between 
closely related isoforms of the same precursor, and consequently assigns the 
same measured abundance to all of them.

feature importance rankings in tree-based models.

The deduplication was applied by grouping features according to their base
identifier and retaining only those with a unique expression vector. This step
reduced the miRNA feature count from \textbf{888 to 442}, eliminating 446
redundant features without discarding any unique biological signal.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Handling Missing Values \& Low Representation Classes}
\label{subsec:missing_values}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Following the elimination of high-missingness columns, a small residual
number of samples still contained at least one missing value across the
remaining clinical features. Specifically, the affected fields and their
missing-value counts were: \texttt{FRACTION\_GENOME\_ALTERED} ($n = 2$),
\texttt{HER2\_STATUS} ($n = 1$), \texttt{METASTASIS\_CODED} ($n = 4$),
\texttt{TUMOR\_T1\_CODED} ($n = 3$), and \texttt{METASTASIS} ($n = 4$).
The miRNA expression matrix itself contained no missing values.

Deciding how to handle missing data is never a purely technical choice: the
appropriate strategy depends on the nature of the data, the mechanism
generating the missingness, and the downstream use. In computational studies
applied to purely numerical or synthetic data, some imputation methods such as
mean substitution, k-nearest neighbour imputation, or generative approaches
like MICE~\cite{buuren2011mice} are standard and often effective. In a
biological dataset such as this one, however, the situation is qualitatively
different. Clinical measurements such as metastasis status, HER2 assessment,
or genomic alteration fraction reflect real, heterogeneous biological processes
in individual patients. Filling in a missing HER2 status with the cohort mean,
or inferring a missing metastasis record from neighbouring samples, risks
fabricating a clinical picture that does not correspond to any real biological
state. Imputed values could mislead the model into learning artificial patterns
(in effect, training on synthetic patients that never existed). Given the
already limited size of the cohort, introducing even a small number of
biologically implausible observations could meaningfully distort the learned
decision boundaries.

Given that the number of affected samples is small relative to the overall cohort, 
and given the potential for imputed values to introduce bias, the affected samples 
were removed via \textbf{listwise deletion}. The miRNA expression matrix contained no missing values, so this
step exclusively affected rows with incomplete clinical annotations. In total,
28 samples were removed, \textbf{reducing the cohort from 272 to 251 samples.} This
represents a loss of 10.3\% of the working cohort, a non-trivial reduction,
but one that is justified by the imperative of data integrity in a biomedical
context. In addition, at the encoding stage described below, the 5 remaining
Normal-like samples were excluded from the supervised experiments due to their
insufficient representation for reliable learning and evaluation, bringing the
final modelling cohort to \textbf{244 samples} across four PAM50 subtypes:
Luminal A ($n = 103$), Luminal B ($n = 71$), Basal-like ($n = 41$), and
HER2-enriched ($n = 29$).

\subsection{Categorical Encoding}
\label{subsec:encoding}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
Machine learning algorithms operate on numerical representations: they compute
distances, gradients, and split criteria over real-valued feature spaces.
Categorical variables, those that take a finite set of discrete named
values rather than continuous numbers, cannot be directly ingested by most
learning algorithms without first being converted to a numerical form. Among
the 20 retained clinical features, 11 are categorical and required explicit
encoding, while the remaining 9 are already numerical and required no
transformation: \texttt{CN\_CLUSTER}, \texttt{FRACTION\_GENOME\_ALTERED},
\texttt{METHYLATION\_CLUSTER}, \texttt{MIRNA\_CLUSTER},
\texttt{MUTATION\_COUNT}, \texttt{SIGCLUST\_UNSUPERVISED\_MRNA},
\texttt{TMB\_NONSYNONYMOUS}, \texttt{AGE}, and \texttt{OS\_MONTHS}.

Two fundamentally different types of categorical variables appear in this
dataset, and they call for different encoding strategies:

\textbf{Ordinal variables} carry a natural rank ordering among their
categories: the categories are not merely distinct names but lie on a
meaningful scale. For these variables, integer codes were assigned to
\textit{preserve the natural clinical ordering}, so that the numerical
distance between codes reflects the conceptual distance between categories.
For example, \texttt{NODES} (lymph node involvement) follows the TNM
classification N0 $<$ N1 $<$ N2 $<$ N3 in order of increasing severity, and
was encoded accordingly as $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$. Similarly, \texttt{TUMOR\_STAGE}
was encoded as T1$\to$1, T2$\to$2, T3$\to$3, T4$\to$4 to preserve the
size-based ordering.

\textbf{Nominal variables} have categories that are qualitatively distinct but
carry no inherent ordering. For binary nominal variables (such as
\texttt{METASTASIS\_CODED} (Negative/Positive), \texttt{NODE\_CODED},
\texttt{METASTASIS} (M0/M1), and \texttt{OS\_STATUS} (0:LIVING/1:DECEASED))
standard 0/1 integer encoding was applied, since with only two categories
the choice of which value maps to 0 or 1 is arbitrary and carries no
structural implication. For multi-valued nominal variables with no meaningful
ordering, a judgment call was required. \texttt{ER\_STATUS} and
\texttt{PR\_STATUS}, for instance, each take four values
(Negative, Positive, Performed but Not Available, Indeterminate), and
\texttt{HER2\_STATUS} takes three (Negative, Equivocal, Positive). These were
encoded with manual integer mappings. It should be noted that for
\texttt{HER2\_STATUS}, there is a partial clinical ordering
(Negative $<$ Equivocal $<$ Positive), and the encoding 0/1/2 was chosen to
reflect this.

\subsection{Train/Test Split and Prevention of Data Leakage}
\label{subsec:split}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The final processing step before model training was the partition of the
dataset into a training set and a held-out test set, followed by feature
standardisation. The order in which these operations are performed is not a
minor implementation detail: it is a methodologically critical design decision
that determines whether reported performance metrics are trustworthy estimates
of generalisation ability or inflated artefacts of inadvertent information
leakage.

This partitioning serves as the primary defense against \textbf{data leakage}, 
which occurs when information from outside the training set is used during the 
learning process. Leakage is a frequent source of overly optimistic performance 
estimates, as the model inadvertently learns patterns from data that would be 
unavailable at inference time. In this pipeline, the prevention of leakage was 
strictly enforced, particularly regarding \textbf{scaling leakage}. This common 
pitfall manifests if a \textit{StandardScaler} is fitted on the full dataset before the 
split; in such cases, the scaling parameters (the mean $\mu$ and standard 
deviation $\sigma$ per feature) would be derived from both training and test 
samples. By ensuring the split occurs before any statistic-based transformation, 
the test set remains truly unseen, preserving the independence assumption 
required for a meaningful evaluation.

To prevent this, the following protocol was strictly enforced throughout all
experiments:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The 80/20 stratified split was performed \textbf{first}, before any
    scaling parameter was computed.

    \item A \textit{StandardScaler} was then fitted \textbf{exclusively on the
    training fold}, computing the per-feature mean $\mu_{\text{train}}$ and
    standard deviation $\sigma_{\text{train}}$ from training samples only.

    \item The transformation derived from the training fold was then applied
    \textbf{without re-fitting} to both the training and test folds:
    \begin{equation}
        z = \frac{x - \mu_{\text{train}}}{\sigma_{\text{train}}}
        \label{eq:zscore}
    \end{equation}
    where $z$ is the standardised feature value and $x$ is the original value.
    The test fold is thus transformed using training statistics, simulating the
    situation at deployment time where the model encounters data whose
    distribution it has not observed.
\end{enumerate}

Standardisation was applied to two groups of features: the five continuous
clinical variables with heterogeneous scales (\texttt{MUTATION\_COUNT},
\texttt{SIGCLUST\_UNSUPERVISED\_MRNA}, \texttt{TMB\_NONSYNONYMOUS},
\texttt{AGE}, \texttt{OS\_MONTHS}), and all 442 miRNA expression columns.
The remaining clinical features, those already encoded as small integers
(representing ordinal or binary categories) were left unscaled as their
values are already bounded and comparable in magnitude.

Raw miRNA expression values span the range $[-2.37,\, 18.66]$ on the
log$_2$ scale, with a mean of $4.97$ and a standard deviation of $4.03$.
Clinical continuous features operate on entirely different numerical scales:
\texttt{MUTATION\_COUNT} ranges from 10 to 223, \texttt{OS\_MONTHS} from 0 to
234, and \texttt{FRACTION\_GENOME\_ALTERED} from 0 to 0.95. Without
standardisation, algorithms sensitive to feature magnitude, such as
distance-based methods and gradient-based optimisers, would disproportionately
weight high-magnitude features irrespective of their actual predictive value.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{What Is Supervised Learning?}
\label{subsec:supervised_intro}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Supervised learning is the branch of machine learning concerned with learning a
mapping from inputs to outputs when labelled training examples are available. Given
a dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$, where $\mathbf{x}_i
\in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a feature vector and $y_i \in \mathcal{Y}$ is the corresponding
label, the goal is to find a function $f : \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}$ that
minimises a loss over the training set and generalises well to unseen examples
\cite{bishop2006pattern, hastie2009elements}. In the classification setting,
$\mathcal{Y}$ is a finite set of discrete categories; in the regression setting it
is a continuous range. In this work, $\mathcal{Y} = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ corresponding
to the four PAM50 subtypes (Basal-like, HER2-enriched, Luminal~A, Luminal~B), so
the problem is a multi-class classification task.

The availability of PAM50 subtype annotations for all patients in the working
cohort is what makes the supervised approach possible and appropriate here. Those
labels are not arbitrary --- they are clinically validated gold-standard subtype
assignments. Supervised learning can therefore directly optimise for the task of
reproducing these assignments, something that unsupervised approaches are unable to
do by construction. Beyond subtype prediction, the supervised framework serves a
second purpose in this work: once a high-performing classifier has been trained, the
internal structure of that model --- specifically, the importance it assigns to
individual features --- is used as a proxy to discover which miRNAs are most
discriminative for each subtype. This dual role (classification + biomarker
discovery) motivates all methodological choices described in the sections that follow.


% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Models Evaluated}
\label{subsec:models}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Four discriminative classifiers and one latent variable integration method were
evaluated in this study. The discriminative classifiers were selected to span a
range of complexity and interpretability, enabling a principled comparison of how
different modelling assumptions interact with the high-dimensional, low-sample-size
structure of the dataset. The latent variable method (DIABLO) was included as a
competitive multi-omics baseline representative of the current state of the art
in integrative cancer subtyping. A brief description of each approach is provided
below.

\paragraph{Decision Tree.}
A Decision Tree recursively partitions the feature space by selecting, at each
internal node, the split that maximally reduces the Gini impurity of the resulting
child partitions. The prediction for a new sample is determined by routing it down
the tree to a leaf and returning the majority class. Decision Trees are fully
interpretable and computationally efficient, but are well-known to exhibit high
variance: small perturbations in the training data can produce substantially different
tree structures. Their inclusion here serves primarily as an interpretable lower-bound
baseline, against which the benefit of ensemble and regularised approaches can be
measured.

\paragraph{Naive Bayes.}
Gaussian Naive Bayes models the class-conditional distribution of each feature as an
independent Gaussian, and applies Bayes' theorem to compute posterior class
probabilities. The conditional independence assumption is the defining characteristic
of this model and simultaneously its principal limitation in the miRNA context, where
widespread co-regulation between miRNA families means independence is systematically
violated. Naive Bayes was included to quantify the cost of this assumption: its
expected underperformance relative to discriminative models is itself informative,
confirming that the dependencies between miRNA features carry discriminative
information that cannot be discarded.

\paragraph{Logistic Regression.}
The multinomial Logistic Regression classifier models the posterior probability of
each class directly from the input features via the softmax function, without
assumptions about the data distribution. It was trained with $\ell_2$ regularisation
(\texttt{penalty='l2'}, \texttt{solver='lbfgs'}) to prevent overfitting in the
high-dimensional feature space. Its principal strength in this context is
interpretability: the weight vector $\mathbf{w}_k$ for each class $k$ directly
encodes the per-feature contribution to the log-odds of that class, making it a
natural companion for cross-validating the feature importance rankings obtained from
XGBoost.

\paragraph{XGBoost.}
XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting)~\cite{chen2016xgboost} is a regularised
implementation of the gradient-boosted decision tree framework. Trees are constructed
sequentially, with each new tree trained to correct the residual errors left by the
current ensemble, scaled by a learning rate $\eta$. The model is trained by minimising
a second-order Taylor approximation of the multinomial log-loss, with explicit
regularisation of both tree complexity and leaf weights. The configuration used
throughout is: \texttt{n\_estimators=50}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.3},
\texttt{max\_depth=10}, \texttt{subsample=0.85}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree=1.0},
\texttt{eval\_metric='mlogloss'}, \texttt{random\_state=42}. This configuration
was selected through a grid search exploration described in
Section~\ref{subsec:gridsearch}. XGBoost was chosen as the primary model for two
reasons: its empirically strong performance on high-dimensional tabular data in
low-sample-size regimes, and its ability to expose per-class feature importance
scores through its internal boosting structure --- the property exploited in the
biomarker discovery pipeline of Section~\ref{subsec:feature_importance}.

\paragraph{DIABLO.}
DIABLO (Data Integration Analysis for Biomarker discovery using Latent cOmponents)
\cite{singh2019diablo} is a multi-omics integration method implemented in the
\texttt{mixOmics} R package. Unlike the discriminative models above, DIABLO is a
latent variable method: it seeks correlated components across data modalities that
jointly maximise discrimination between groups, projecting the data into a shared
latent space where class structure is maximally preserved. DIABLO was included as a
competitive baseline representative of the state of the art in multi-omics breast
cancer subtyping, enabling a principled comparison between latent-representation and
direct discriminative approaches. The implementation followed the official TCGA case
study~\cite{diabloTCGA}, adapted to the four-class PAM50 problem and the specific
feature structure of the working cohort. Two DIABLO configurations were evaluated:
\textit{Single Block} (miRNA data only, mirroring the homogeneous setting of the
discriminative models) and \textit{All Blocks} (miRNA + clinical data, mirroring
the heterogeneous setting).

\subsection{Impact of Clinical Data: Statistical Validation}
\label{subsec:clinical_impact}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The performance differences observed in the single-split results are suggestive but
not statistically conclusive. To rigorously test whether the addition of clinical
features produces a significant improvement over miRNA-only classification, a
Repeated Stratified K-Fold cross-validation experiment was conducted using XGBoost

StandardScaler was fitted exclusively on the training partition and applied to both
partitions, ensuring strict leakage prevention throughout. The macro F1-score was
recorded for both configurations at every fold, and the resulting paired distributions
were compared using the \textbf{Wilcoxon signed-rank test} --- a non-parametric
paired test that does not assume normality of the score distribution and is therefore
well suited to comparing performance across repeated folds.

\subsection{XGBoost as the Primary Model}
\label{subsec:xgb_selection}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Across both feature configurations and all evaluation metrics, XGBoost consistently
outperforms the other discriminative models and substantially surpasses DIABLO.
Three properties of XGBoost make it particularly well-suited to the structure of this
problem and motivate its selection as the primary model for the biomarker discovery
pipeline.

First, XGBoost is well-calibrated for high-dimensional, low-sample-size settings.
With $n=195$ training samples and $p=462$ features ($p/n \approx 2.4$), the risk of
overfitting is non-trivial. XGBoost's built-in regularisation mechanisms --- the
learning rate shrinkage, column subsampling at each split, and the explicit
regularisation of tree complexity --- collectively reduce generalisation error without
requiring a separate dimensionality reduction step.

Second, XGBoost's sequential boosting strategy explicitly focuses learning effort on
samples that the current ensemble misclassifies. This is particularly beneficial for
the minority subtypes (HER2-enriched, Basal-like) whose small class sizes would
otherwise receive disproportionately little gradient signal in a standard
single-pass classifier.

Third, and most critically for the downstream application in this work, XGBoost
provides two complementary mechanisms for per-class feature importance extraction:
(i) the multi-class booster's internal tree structure, which assigns trees to classes
by interleaving, enabling class-specific feature importance to be recovered; and
(ii) binary one-vs-rest booster analysis, where a separate binary XGBoost is trained
per subtype and the gain-based importance is extracted from its booster directly.
Both mechanisms are exploited in Section~\ref{subsec:feature_importance}.

\subsection{Feature Importance and miRNA Biomarker Discovery}
\label{subsec:feature_importance}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

Having established XGBoost as the primary model, the next objective is to use it as
a proxy instrument for discovering which miRNAs are most discriminative for each
PAM50 subtype. This requires a careful choice of problem formulation, because the
question ``which features are most important for subtype $k$?'' can be interpreted in
two fundamentally different ways, each requiring a different experimental design.

\subsubsection*{Two Problem Formulations}

\paragraph{Formulation 1 — Multi-class: What distinguishes the subtypes from each other?}

In the multi-class setting, a single XGBoost model is trained to simultaneously
classify all four subtypes. XGBoost's gradient boosting procedure assigns trees to
classes in a round-robin fashion: in a model with $T$ estimators and $K = 4$ classes,
tree indices $\{0, 4, 8, \ldots\}$ are assigned to class 0, indices $\{1, 5, 9,
\ldots\}$ to class 1, and so on. By extracting and aggregating the feature usage
frequencies across only the trees belonging to class $k$, a per-class feature
importance ranking can be recovered from the multi-class model. This formulation
answers the question: \textit{``which features drive the boundaries between all
four subtypes jointly?''} and is appropriate for understanding the global structure
of the feature space.

\paragraph{Formulation 2 — Binary one-vs-rest: What characterises this specific subtype?}

In the binary formulation, a separate XGBoost model is trained for each subtype $k$
in a one-vs-rest manner: the target is binarised as $y_i^{(k)} = \mathbf{1}[y_i =
k]$, so the model learns to distinguish subtype $k$ from the union of all other
subtypes. The feature importance is extracted from the booster of this binary model
using the \textbf{gain} metric --- the average improvement in the loss function
brought by a feature across all splits where it is used. This formulation answers a
different question: \textit{``what is the unique molecular signature of this specific
subtype, relative to everything else?''} Unlike the multi-class approach, the binary
model is not influenced by the difficulty of discriminating between the other three
subtypes; it is entirely focused on what makes subtype $k$ special.

The choice between the two formulations is not merely technical --- it reflects a
scientific question. In this work, both formulations are applied:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{multi-class formulation} is used to characterise the relative
    difficulty of discriminating between the four subtypes and to understand global
    feature contribution patterns.
    \item The \textbf{binary one-vs-rest formulation} is used as the primary approach
    for miRNA biomarker discovery, since it is designed to isolate the features that
    uniquely characterise each subtype against all others --- the most clinically
    relevant question for biomarker identification.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Separability Analysis}

A critical observation that links the classification results to the feature importance
findings is the pronounced asymmetry in how well each subtype is characterised by its
top features. Table~\ref{tab:feature_importances} reveals that the top 20 features
account for approximately 98.7\% of total gain for Basal-like, but only 58.4\% for
Luminal~A and 51.4\% for Luminal~B. This concentration of gain in a small number of
features is a direct reflection of how well-defined and biologically distinct each
subtype's molecular signature is.

For Basal-like, a single feature --- MIR-577/577 --- accounts for 56.86\% of the
total booster gain, an extraordinary concentration that reflects the uniquely
distinctive molecular identity of this subtype among the four classes. Basal-like
tumours, also known as triple-negative breast cancers, have a fundamentally different
transcriptional landscape from the three Luminal and HER2 subtypes, and MIR-577/577
appears to be a primary molecular marker of this distinction in the expression data.
This is consistent with the perfect classification results (F1 = 1.0) achieved by
all models for this subtype.

HER2-enriched presents a more distributed importance profile: the top feature
(MIR-30/30A) accounts for only 10.65\% of gain, and importance is spread across a
broader set of features. This diffuse signal is consistent with the classification
results, where HER2-enriched was the hardest subtype to classify --- the absence of
a dominant discriminative feature means the model must rely on a combination of many
weaker signals. Notably, \texttt{HER2\_STATUS} appears in the top 10 for this
subtype (rank 10, 2.85\% gain) when clinical data is included, confirming the
expected alignment between the clinical marker and the molecular subtype assignment
and providing a useful sanity check for the model's internal reasoning.

For Luminal~A and Luminal~B, the importance is similarly distributed, with no single
miRNA dominating. This is expected: the Luminal subtypes are the most closely related
of the four, sharing hormone receptor positivity and overlapping expression programmes,
and their discrimination is inherently a harder problem. The appearance of
MIR-205/205* in the top 10 of both Luminal~A and Luminal~B (ranks 9 and 9 respectively)
is particularly noteworthy, as it suggests this miRNA contributes to Luminal identity
broadly rather than being specific to one subtype --- a finding that warrants further
biological investigation.

\subsubsection*{Classification Performance on Reduced Feature Sets}

To validate that the top features identified by the binary booster are genuinely
sufficient to characterise each subtype --- and not merely artefacts of feature
importance rankings --- the following experiment was conducted for each subtype $k$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The top-$K$ features identified by the binary booster for subtype $k$ were
    extracted (panels of $K = 3$ and $K = 10$ were evaluated).
    \item A new multi-class XGBoost classifier was trained using only those $K$ features.
    \item The per-class F1-score for subtype $k$ was compared between the full-feature
    model and the reduced-feature model.
    \item A paired t-test across multiple cross-validation configurations
    (\texttt{n\_splits} $\in \{3, 5, 7, 10\}$, \texttt{n\_repeats} $\in \{1, 5, 7,
    10\}$) was used to assess whether the performance difference was statistically
    significant.
\end{enumerate}

The results confirm the sufficiency of the selected panels for Basal-like: a panel
of just 3 features (MIR-577/577, MIR-934/934, MIR-135B/135B*) achieves F1 = 1.0 on

no statistically significant degradation for the majority of CV configurations. For
HER2-enriched, Luminal~A, and Luminal~B, the reduced panels do incur a statistically
significant performance drop (confirmed by $p < 0.05$ in most CV configurations),
indicating that the broader feature set contributes meaningful information for these
harder subtypes and that a minimal panel is insufficient to fully characterise them.
This is consistent with the distributed importance profiles observed for these
subtypes.
